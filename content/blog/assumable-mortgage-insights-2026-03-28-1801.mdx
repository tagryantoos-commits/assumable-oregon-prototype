Alright. Let's talk about where we actually are right now with assumable mortgages in 2026.

I've been doing this long enough to know that people hear "assumable mortgage" and think either A) it's a scam, B) it's too good to be true, or C) they don't qualify. Sometimes all three. So let me cut through the noise and give you the real picture of what's happening in the market.

## The Rate Environment Right Now

Here's the thing. Rates have moved around a lot over the past couple years. Some sellers locked in rates at 2.65%, 2.75%, maybe even lower if they bought during the peak of the market. Other sellers have rates in the 5% range from more recent purchases.

What matters: if a seller has a 3% rate and you assume their loan, you're stepping into that 3% rate. Your lender doesn't get to change it. It's locked in from the original purchase.

Compare that to going out and getting a new mortgage today. Depending on market conditions, you could be looking at 6.5%, 7%, even higher. So yeah. The math can look really, really good.

Let's say you're assuming a $400,000 loan at 3% over 30 years. Your payment lands around $1,686 a month. Go out and get a fresh $400,000 mortgage at 6.5%? You're at roughly $2,530 a month. That's $844 more per month. Over 30 years, that's just over $303,000 in extra payments.

That's not hype. That's math.

## Down Payment Reality Check

One reason people get excited about assumptions is the down payment piece. You don't always need 20%. Some assumptions let you come in with 5%, 10%, even less depending on the loan type and your situation.

So if you're looking at a $500,000 property and you assume a $400,000 loan, you're down to needing $100,000 in cash. Still a chunk. But if you were buying that same property with a traditional mortgage, you'd need more. The assumable path can absolutely be lighter on the down payment.

Now, real talk. Getting approved to assume isn't automatic. The lender is going to underwrite you. They want to know your credit score, your debt-to-income ratio, your employment history. It's not as simple as "oh, the old owner had a loan, so I'll just take it." The bank has to say yes first.

If you've got solid credit and reasonable debt, you're in good shape. If you're borderline, it can get tricky.

## The Assumption Costs You Need to Budget For

Here's what trips people up. They see the low rate and think it's free money. It's not.

You've got appraisal fees (typically $400-700). Title work and insurance (varies, but plan on $1,000-2,000). Loan assumption fees from the lender (usually 0.5% to 1% of the loan amount). In some cases, property taxes, HOA transfers, inspections.

Add it up and you could be looking at $3,000-5,000 in closing costs depending on the loan size and your situation. Not huge, but not nothing. Budget for it.

And here's the thing I want to be clear about: not all loans are assumable. FHA loans, VA loans, USDA loans? Yeah, those are assumable. Conventional loans backed by Fannie Mae or Freddie Mac? Usually not. You need to know what you're dealing with before you get your hopes up. We've got [a guide on assuming FHA mortgages](/blog/how-to-assume-fha-mortgage-step-by-step) if that's the loan type you're looking at.

## The Assumption vs. New Mortgage Trade-Off

So when does an assumption actually make sense?

If the seller has a significantly lower rate than what's available in the market right now, and you qualify to assume, and the property is actually worth what you're paying. Those are the big three.

But it's not always the slam dunk everyone thinks it is. Some assumptions fall apart during underwriting. Some sellers ask for a premium because they know their rate is valuable (and honestly, sometimes that premium wipes out your savings). Some properties aren't assumable because of due-on-sale clauses or lender restrictions.

The real story is this: you have to run the numbers on every deal individually. What are the rates? What are your closing costs? How long are you staying? What would a new loan cost you?

I see a lot of people fixated on "getting that low rate" without actually doing the math on the full picture. That's backwards.

## Where Assumptions Shine

They work great when:

1. You find a property with a genuinely good rate locked in (sub-4%, ideally).
2. The current market rate is notably higher (5%+).
3. You can qualify and the underwriting goes smooth.
4. You're going to stay in the property for at least 5-7 years.

If all of that lines up, an assumption can save you serious money. Tens of thousands, sometimes more.

They're less exciting when:

1. The rate advantage is minimal (a 4.5% assumption vs. a 4.8% new mortgage? Not enough to matter).
2. The seller wants a big premium to cover the difference in rate.
3. You don't have the down payment ready.
4. You don't have solid credit or a strong DTI ratio.

## What's Actually Happening in Colorado Right Now

Colorado's market is interesting because you've got sellers from different points in time. Someone who bought in 2020 or 2021? They likely have a killer rate and might be willing to let it assume. Someone who bought in 2024? Their rate's already high, so the incentive to sell with an assumption is lower.

In Colorado Springs specifically, we're seeing interest in assumptions from buyers who are relocating here for work and need to move fast. An assumption can close quicker than a traditional mortgage in some cases because there's less back-and-forth with underwriting. That matters when you've got a timeline.

I've also noticed more sellers mentioning assumptions as a selling point because they know it appeals to buyers who are rate-conscious. That's good awareness on their part. It's also a reason to understand the real value before you get too excited about it.

## The Real Talk

Assumptions are a legitimate tool. They're not for everyone. They're not a shortcut that works on every deal. But when the numbers line up, they can be a really smart move.

The key is doing your homework. Run the numbers. Get pre-approval. Know what you qualify for. Understand the loan type you're assuming. Don't fall in love with a property because of a rate advantage that doesn't actually cash out to real savings once you factor everything in.
