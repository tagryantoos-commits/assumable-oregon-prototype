Alright, so here's the thing. I get this question constantly: "Ryan, can I assume an FHA loan? What about conventional?" And the answer isn't as simple as yes or no.

Both can be assumed. But they work totally differently. The rules are different. The costs are different. And honestly, one of them is going to make way more sense for your situation than the other.

Let me walk you through exactly how each one works so you can figure out which path makes sense for you in 2026.

## FHA Assumptions: The Easier Path (Usually)

FHA loans are assumable. Full stop. That's the good news.

Here's why this matters: FHA doesn't care if you're the original borrower or not. As long as you qualify, you can take over that loan. No lender approval required in the traditional sense. The seller's lender just has to be notified.

The down payment requirement for an FHA assumption is 3.5% of the purchase price. So if you're buying a 400K house with an assumable FHA, you need about 14K down. That's pretty manageable.

But here's where it gets tricky. There are a couple of things you need to know about FHA assumptions.

**The mortgage insurance stays.** If the original loan had mortgage insurance (PMI), it's still there. You can't get rid of it unless you refinance out of the FHA loan entirely. That's a big deal because that insurance is costing the seller extra money every month. It gets passed to you. If the original loan had a 0.85% annual mortgage insurance premium, you're paying that too.

**You still have to qualify.** The lender's going to run your credit, check your debt-to-income ratio, verify employment. All the normal mortgage stuff. You need a decent credit score and solid income. If you don't qualify, the deal doesn't happen.

**The appraisal is required.** Some people think FHA assumptions bypass the appraisal. Nope. You're still getting an appraisal. It has to meet current FHA standards too. That costs 500 to 800 bucks.

But here's the biggest benefit: FHA loans have been around forever. There are tons of them in Colorado. Tons. If you're looking for an assumable loan right now, you're probably going to find FHA first.

## Conventional Assumptions: Rarer, But Sometimes Better

Conventional loans are assumable too. But not all of them.

This is important. A conventional loan is only assumable if the original lender allows it. You have to check the loan documents. Some conventional loans have a "due-on-sale" clause that prevents assumption entirely. Others allow it freely. Most fall somewhere in the middle.

Here's the thing that makes conventional assumptions interesting: they often come with better rates.

Conventional loans originated before rates tanked have rates you can't get today. If someone locked in a 2.85% conventional loan back in 2020, and rates are sitting at 5.5% right now, that's a 2.65% rate difference. On a 400K loan, that saves you roughly 850 bucks a month. Over 30 years, that's over 300K in savings.

That's not an exaggeration. That's math.

But conventional assumptions have a higher bar. The lender's going to vet you hard. They want to make sure you can actually carry the loan. Your debt-to-income ratio, credit score, income verification, all of it gets scrutinized. And the lender can deny the assumption if they don't think you qualify. It's rare, but it happens.

Down payment on a conventional assumption is typically 10-20% depending on the loan and the lender. So you'd need 40K to 80K down on that 400K house. That's steeper than FHA, but if you're getting a killer rate, it might be worth it.

## Here's What Makes This Decision Real

Let me give you a scenario.

You find a 450K house in Colorado Springs. It has an assumable FHA loan at 4.2% with 25 years remaining. You'd need about 16K down. Your payment (principal and interest) would be around 2,200 a month before taxes and insurance.

Same house. Different scenario. It has an assumable conventional at 2.9% with 28 years remaining. Down payment is 60K. Your payment drops to about 1,500 a month.

That's 700 bucks a month. Seven hundred. Over the life of the loan, you're looking at over 235K in additional savings.

But you need 60K instead of 16K. That's a 44K difference in cash at close.

This is where you have to sit down and figure out what makes sense for your situation. Do you have the cash? Are you willing to spend it for the lower payment and long-term savings? Or do you need to keep cash on hand and take the FHA route with a slightly higher rate?

## The Qualification Piece

Here's something a lot of people miss: conventional assumptions can be harder to qualify for because the lender's more strict. FHA is actually more flexible in some ways, especially if you have a lower credit score or higher debt-to-income ratio.

If you're at the edge of qualifying, FHA might be your only play. And honestly, that's fine. An assumable FHA at 4.2% beats buying at 5.5% in today's market.

## What About VA Loans?

Quick side note: VA loans are assumable and they're incredible if you qualify. You can assume a VA loan with zero down if you're eligible. The rates are usually competitive. But you have to be a veteran or active duty. Both FHA and conventional assumptions are real tools in Colorado in 2026. FHA is more common and easier to qualify for. Conventional can offer better rates but requires more cash down and stricter approval.
