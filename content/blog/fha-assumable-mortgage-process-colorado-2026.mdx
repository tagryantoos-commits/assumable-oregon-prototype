# FHA Assumable Mortgage Process in Colorado: Step-by-Step Guide for 2026

Alright. Let's be real for a second. The FHA assumable mortgage process isn't magic. It's not even that complicated once you break it down. But a lot of people overthink it because nobody's walking them through what actually happens.

So here's what I'm going to do. I'm going to take you step-by-step through the entire thing. No fluff. Just what you need to know to actually get this done in Colorado.

## Why FHA Assumable Mortgages Even Matter

Quick context before we dive in. An FHA assumable mortgage means you're taking over someone else's loan. You're not refinancing. You're not getting a new loan from a new lender. You're literally stepping into their shoes with their interest rate, their payment, their timeline.

Here's the financial part that matters. If someone locked in a 3.5% rate in 2022 and rates are sitting at 7% right now, you're looking at saving somewhere around $200 to $400 a month depending on the loan size. On a $300,000 loan, that's $2,400 to $4,800 per year. Over 10 years, you do the math.

That's why people care about this.

## Step 1: Find a Property with an Assumable FHA Loan

You can't assume a loan that doesn't exist. So step one is finding a house that has an FHA mortgage the seller is actually willing to let you assume.

Here's the thing though. Not all sellers advertise this. Some don't even know their loan is assumable. That's where we come in. When we're looking for properties, we're checking the loan documents. We're asking questions the right way. We know what we're looking for.

If you're searching on your own, ask the listing agent directly. "Is this loan assumable?" A lot of agents don't think to mention it because they're not trained on it. But it's a legitimate feature just like a garage or a basement.

Pro tip: FHA loans originated after 1986 are assumable with lender approval. Most loans out there fall into this category. So there's more inventory out there than you think.

## Step 2: Get Pre-Qualification from the Lender

Once you find a property, you need to reach out to the current lender. Not your lender. Their lender.

This is important. You call the servicer of the loan (the company collecting the payments). You tell them you want to assume the mortgage. They're going to ask for some basic info about you. Credit score range. Income. Down payment you can bring.

They'll give you a rough idea of whether you even qualify. This isn't a full underwriting. It's just a "okay, we think this is possible" conversation.

Why do this early? Because if the lender says no, you need to know that before you spend money on inspections or appraisals.

## Step 3: Make an Offer with Assumption Language

Here's where a lot of people mess up. They make a regular offer and then try to figure out the assumption piece later. No. The assumption goes in the contract from day one.

Your offer needs to include language that says something like this: "This offer is contingent on the buyer's ability to assume the existing FHA mortgage currently held by [Lender Name]."

This protects you. If the lender won't approve the assumption, you can walk away without losing your earnest money.

Also in the offer: you're going to specify down payment amount. Sometimes it's just the difference between the home's value and the remaining loan balance. Sometimes the seller will ask for more. That's negotiable.

## Step 4: Get a Home Inspection and Appraisal

Standard stuff here, but there's an assumption twist.

The lender is going to order an appraisal. They want to make sure the house is worth at least what's owed on the loan. If it's not, they can deny the assumption. (This is rare in Colorado's market, but it happens.)

The home inspection is all you. You're checking for problems. You're not assuming someone else's bad surprises.

Timeline wise: 7 to 10 days for the appraisal. Inspection can happen faster.

## Step 5: Complete the Full Loan Application

Now the lender gets serious. They're pulling your full financial picture.

You'll need:
- Last two months of pay stubs
- Last two years of tax returns
- Bank statements
- Asset documentation
- Employment verification
- Complete credit authorization

This is where they actually decide if you qualify. They're looking at your debt-to-income ratio. They're checking your credit score. FHA typically wants a 580 credit score minimum, but stronger is better.

This takes 5 to 7 business days if you get everything in on time. If you're slow, it stretches longer.

## Step 6: Lender Approval and Clear to Close

Assuming your financials check out, the lender issues "clear to close." This means they're approving the assumption.

Here's what gets tricky. The lender is going to want certain things in writing. They might require proof you can cover closing costs. They might ask for updated financial statements. Some lenders are stricter than others.

Colorado lenders we work with regularly move pretty smoothly through this. But I've seen lenders from other states drag their feet because they're not familiar with assumptions.

Budget 3 to 5 business days for this phase.

## Step 7: Title Search and Insurance

You need title insurance just like any other purchase. The title company is going to search the property's history to make sure there are no liens or claims against it.

If everything's clean, you're good. If there are issues, they get resolved before closing.

This happens simultaneously with lender approval. Takes about 5 to 7 business days.

## Step 8: Final Walkthrough and Closing

Two to three days before closing, do a final walkthrough. Make sure repairs are done if any were negotiated. Make sure the house is in the condition you agreed to.

At closing, you're signing documents. A lot of documents. The assumption paperwork, title documents, disclosure forms, payment arrangements.

Closing typically takes 45 minutes to an hour. You'll bring a cashier's check for your down payment and closing costs. Or you can wire funds depending on the title company's preference.

After you sign, the lender records the documents. Usually happens same day.

You get the keys. You own the thing.

## Timeline: How Long Does This Actually Take?

Start to finish. About 30 to 45 days if everything runs clean.

- Offer to appraisal approval: 10 days
- Appraisal to application complete: 7 days
- Application processing: 7 to 10 days
- Clear to close: 3 to 5 days
- Closing: 1 to 2 days

If anyone's slow (which happens), add another two weeks.

## What This Actually Costs

Closing costs on an assumption run 2% to 3% of the loan amount. So on a $300,000 loan, you're looking at $6,000 to $9,000.

That includes the appraisal, title insurance, recording fees, your attorney (if you use one), and lender fees.

Sometimes the seller covers part of this. Sometimes you do. That's negotiable in the offer.

Down payment is separate. That's whatever you agreed to in the contract.

## Common Reasons Assumptions Get Denied

I want to be straight with you. Not every assumption works out.

The lender says no because your credit score is too low. Your debt-to-income ratio is too high. Your income can't support the payment. The appraisal comes in under the loan amount.

Chances of this happening? Honestly, if you're marginally qualified, maybe 20%. If your credit and income are solid, closer to 5%.

## What Makes This Different Than a Refinance

I get asked this all the time. Why assume instead of refinance?

With a refinance, you're getting a brand new loan at today's rates. So if rates are 7%, you're paying 7%. You pay for a full appraisal, title insurance, all the closing costs.

With an assumption, you're keeping the old rate. 3.5%. 4%. Whatever they locked in. You still pay some fees but it's less. And the timeline is faster.

The math almost always wins on the assumption side if the rate gap is significant.
