# How We Turn Assumable Mortgage Leads Into Actual Sales (Without the BS)

Look. I'm going to tell you something most real estate teams won't admit. We get a ton of leads that come in unpitched. Random questions. Market curiosities. People asking about edge cases nobody talks about.

Most teams ignore those. They're "not qualified" or "not a fit" or whatever.

We don't. We turn them into blog posts. And those blog posts are making us sales.

Here's how we actually do it.

## Why Unpitched Leads Are Gold (If You Know What to Do With Them)

So first, let me explain what I mean by "unpitched leads." These aren't people who booked a call and told us their situation. These are questions that come in through our website, emails, social media comments, or random conversations.

"Can you assume a conventional loan?"
"Is there an assumable mortgage market in Savannah?"
"How much money do I actually save assuming a 2.8% rate?"

Real questions. Real intent. But they're not someone saying "I want to buy a house next month."

Here's the thing though. Those questions represent demand. Someone cared enough to ask. They're searching for an answer. And if we're the ones who give them that answer in a format Google can rank, we become the authority.

That authority converts to calls. Those calls convert to contracts.

We started tracking these unpitched questions about three years ago. I'd say we get roughly 14 solid ones every month or so. Topics we could actually write about. Not spam. Not duplicates. Just good material.

So we built a process around it.

## The Process: From Random Question to Published Post

### Step 1: Capture and Tag Everything

We have a simple Google Sheet. When a question comes in, someone on the team logs it.

Not fancy. Just:
- The exact question they asked
- Where it came from (website form, email, social, conversation)
- Geographic angle (if there is one)
- Intent level (1-10, how ready are they to buy?)

Takes 30 seconds. Matters a ton.

Over a month, we look at that sheet. We're looking for patterns. Not every question becomes a post. But if we see three variations of the same question, that's a post.

### Step 2: Research Before Writing

I don't write anything without running the numbers first.

If someone asks "How much can I save assuming a 3.2% rate in a 7% market?" I'm pulling actual comps. I'm calculating monthly payments. I'm showing the math.

Let me give you an example. We did a post called [Can You Assume a Conventional Loan](/blog/can-you-assume-conventional-loan). The lead came in from someone who'd heard rumors but didn't know the real answer.

Before I wrote a single sentence, I researched:
- Current conventional assumability rules
- Success rates for different buyer profiles
- What lenders are actually allowing
- Real transaction examples from our own deals

Then I had something worth writing.

### Step 3: Structure It Like an Actual Conversation

This is where most teams mess up. They write blog posts like they're writing a Wikipedia entry.

I write them like I'm explaining it to you on the phone.

Open with the direct answer. "Yes, you can assume some conventional loans. But here's what actually happens."

Use short sentences. Ask rhetorical questions. Break things into numbered steps instead of paragraphs.

If there's a downside, I lead with it. "Chances of this working for a non-veteran is currently around 20%." Real talk.

Throw in specific numbers. "You'd save roughly $180,000 over the life of a 30-year loan by assuming a 2.8% rate instead of getting a new one at 7.2%." Not "save a lot." That's actual math.

### Step 4: Optimize Without Gaming the System

We're not trying to trick Google. We're just trying to make sure people who are searching for the answer actually find us.

So we research the search terms people are actually using. "Assumable mortgage" is obvious. But what about "How much money can I save with an assumable mortgage?" Or "What is the current assumable mortgage rate?"

We work those into the post naturally. Not stuffed. Just organic. The post should make sense if you took out every SEO tactic.

We also make sure to link to related posts we've already written. If we're talking about conventionals, we link to our post on [Growing Demand for Assumable Mortgages](/blog/growing-demand-assumable-mortgages). If we're discussing a specific market, we link to geographic pieces like [Assumable Mortgage El Paso](/blog/assumable-mortgage-el-paso-texas).

Internal links help Google understand what we know about.

### Step 5: Edit Ruthlessly for Redundancy

Here's where I'm going to be honest about AI content. The biggest tell isn't the words. It's saying the same thing three different ways.

Real people say things once and move on. AI restates constantly.

So after I write, I read it again. Every paragraph. Every transition.

"Are we making this point twice?" If the answer is yes, cut one version.

"Is this sentence supporting something we already said?" Delete it.

We're aiming for concise. Not short. There's a difference. We go long when we need to explain something complex. But we never go long to make something sound more sophisticated.

### Step 6: Publish and Monitor

We publish on our main site. We share it once on social media when it goes live.

Then we monitor. How many people clicked? How long did they spend on the page? Did anyone fill out a form?

If a post isn't getting traction after a month, we look at why. Sometimes it's a rewrite. Sometimes we need to reoptimize the title. Sometimes the demand just wasn't there.

But most of the time? These posts convert. Not immediately. But over six months, a solid post will generate 10 to 15 qualified leads for us.

Multiply that by 14 posts a month, and the math gets interesting.

## Why This Works Better Than Paid Ads

Here's the weird part. I spend way less on content than most teams spend on ads. And I get better quality leads.

Paid ads reach people we target. Content reaches people who are actually searching. There's a difference in intent.

Someone clicking an ad is interested. Someone typing "assumable mortgage rates Colorado Springs" into Google at 11 PM because they're thinking about their financial future? That's a different level of ready.

We've been doing this for about three years now. The compounding effect is real. Posts rank. They get shared. New leads keep coming in.

## What You Should Actually Take From This

If you're in real estate or any business, you have this opportunity too.

You probably get random questions all the time. About your niche. Your market. Your specific expertise.

Don't ignore them. Document them. Turn them into content.

You don't need a massive marketing budget. You need to actually help people. And when you do, they remember. They call. They convert.
