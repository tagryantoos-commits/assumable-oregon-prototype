Here's the thing. FHA assumable mortgages exist. They're legal. They're available right now in Colorado. And most people have no idea they can use them.

If you're seeing homes with 3% interest rates when the market's sitting at 6.5%, that's not luck. That's an assumable mortgage. And I'm going to walk you through exactly what it takes to get one in 2026.

## What You're Actually Buying When You Assume

First, let's be clear on what happens. When you assume an FHA mortgage, you're literally taking over the seller's loan. You get their rate. You get their payment. You keep all their terms. No new loan application with a new bank. No waiting for underwriting. No rate lock drama.

The catch? The bank still has to approve you. And the seller has to agree. But if both of those things line up, you're walking into a home with a payment that might be $400 to $600 a month cheaper than a conventional mortgage on the same property.

Let me throw some real numbers at you. Say you're looking at a home in Colorado Springs listed at $450,000. The seller has an FHA loan at 3.2% with 20 years left on it. Their payment is around $1,850 a month (principal and interest only).

A conventional 30-year mortgage at today's rates (6.5%) on that same home? You're paying $2,852 a month. That's an extra $1,002 per month. Over 20 years, that's $240,480 more.

Now. Does that grab your attention?

## The Basic Requirements (The Stuff That Actually Matters)

Here's what the bank is looking at when you apply to assume an FHA mortgage.

**Credit Score**

You need a minimum 580 FICO score. That's FHA's floor. Most lenders won't touch you below 620, but technically 580 is the requirement. If you're hovering around 580 to 600, you're going to have a harder time. If you're at 620 and above, you're in reasonable shape.

**Debt-to-Income Ratio**

This is the big one. FHA wants to see your total monthly debt divided by your gross monthly income. They'll typically approve you up to 50% DTI, sometimes 55% depending on the lender and your compensating factors.

Let's say you make $5,000 a month gross. Your max debt payment (including the new mortgage) would be $2,500. That includes car payments, student loans, credit cards, the whole thing.

Here's the part people get wrong. They look at the payment of the assumable mortgage and think they're golden. But if you've got a $400 car payment, a $300 student loan payment, and a $600 credit card minimum, you've got $1,300 in existing debt. Add the $1,850 mortgage, and you're at $3,150. That's 63% DTI. The bank says no.

You need to be honest about this number before you fall in love with a property.

**Liquid Assets and Reserves**

FHA wants to see that you can actually close and handle the loan. You'll need enough liquid assets (savings, investments, not retirement accounts) to cover your down payment and closing costs. Most lenders want to see 2 months of the new mortgage payment in reserves after closing. Some want 3.

**Employment and Income Verification**

Two years of tax returns. Pay stubs. W-2s. If you're self-employed, it gets messier. The bank wants to see that your income is stable and likely to continue. If you just switched jobs, they might ask for a letter from your new employer confirming you're hired and stating your salary.

**Valid Social Security Number and Legal Status**

You need to be a U.S. citizen or permanent resident. No exceptions here.

## What Makes an FHA Loan Assumable

Not all FHA loans are assumable. Here's the critical part.

If the FHA loan was issued before December 15, 1989, it's assumable with no lender approval. You and the seller just need to agree. The original borrower stays on the deed of trust (the bank's claim on the property), but you're making the payments.

If the FHA loan was issued after December 15, 1989? The bank gets to approve or deny you. They'll run your credit, check your income, verify everything. And here's the twist. The original borrower can potentially stay liable if you default, depending on the release language in the original note.

In 2026, almost every assumable mortgage you're looking at was issued after 1989. So assume the bank will be involved.

## The Underwriting Process (What to Expect)

Alright. You've found a property. You've negotiated an assumption. Now what happens.

**Step 1. Get Pre-Approval for Assumption**

Contact an FHA lender (not all lenders do assumptions) and ask for a pre-approval. They'll run your credit, verify your income, and give you a preliminary thumbs up or down. This usually takes 3 to 5 business days.

**Step 2. Order the Loan Package**

Your lender requests the original promissory note, the deed of trust, and all loan documents from the servicer. This takes another 5 to 7 days. You need these to understand the exact terms you're assuming.

**Step 3. Full Underwriting**

Your lender digs in. They'll verify every income claim. They'll pull a full tri-merge credit report. They'll get an updated appraisal to confirm the property's current value. They'll review your employment history. This phase takes 7 to 10 business days typically, but it can stretch if they need clarification.

**Step 4. Conditional Approval**

The lender comes back with conditions. Maybe they want a letter of explanation for a late payment from three years ago. Maybe they want proof of a down payment gift from a family member. You address these. Usually takes 3 to 5 business days.

**Step 5. Clear to Close**

Once conditions are satisfied, you get the green light. Your title company prepares the assumption documents. You sign. The seller is released from liability (in most cases). You're now the owner with the new loan terms in place.

The whole process typically takes 30 to 45 days. Not weeks. Not months. Weeks.

## The Down Payment Reality

Here's where it gets interesting and where a lot of misconceptions live.

With an FHA assumable mortgage, you still need to put down money. You're buying equity, not just taking over a payment. If the home is worth $450,000 and the seller's loan balance is $380,000, there's $70,000 in equity. You need to bring cash to the closing table to cover that gap (or the seller can carry back a second mortgage, but that's more complex).

So your out-of-pocket down payment is the difference between the current home value and the remaining loan balance. Not 3%. Not 5%. Just whatever the equity is.

This is actually huge, because if the home has appreciated since the seller bought it, you might be putting down 10% to 25%. If the home hasn't appreciated much, maybe it's 5% to 8%.

But here's the win. Your monthly payment is still locked in at the original rate. Even though you're putting down less than you would on a conventional loan, you're getting a rate that's 2% to 3% lower. The math works out in your favor.

## Approval Odds and Honesty Check

I'm going to be straight with you. If you're a non-veteran, your approval odds right now are in the 20% to 30% range for FHA assumptions. That's not because the bank is being unreasonable. It's because most people don't qualify.

They have too much existing debt. Or their income is too unstable. Or their credit score is too low. Or they haven't been in their job long enough.

If you're a veteran, your odds jump to about 60% to 70%. VA loans are assumable by anyone (no credit requirement, no approval needed), so the pool of veterans is larger.

The point is: know your situation before you get excited about a property. Pull your credit report. Calculate your DTI. Be honest about whether you'll actually qualify.
