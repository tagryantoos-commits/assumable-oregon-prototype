Alright, let's talk VA loan assumptions. If you're military (active duty, reserve, National Guard, or veteran), you've got access to one of the best mortgage products out there. And here's the thing: you can also assume someone else's VA loan.

But there's a lot of confusion around who qualifies, what the actual requirements are, and what happens to the person selling you the house. I'm going to walk you through all of it.

## The Basic Rule: You Need VA Eligibility

Here's the bottom line. To assume a VA loan, you need to have VA eligibility yourself. Full stop.

You're eligible if you're:
- Active duty military (any branch)
- A veteran who served honorably
- A surviving spouse of a service member who died in service or from a service-connected disability
- A reservist or National Guard member (depending on your service)

The VA doesn't care if you've used your VA loan benefit before. You can assume multiple VA loans if you have multiple entitlements. That's actually one of the coolest parts. If you got out, bought a house with your VA loan, and now you're back on active duty in Colorado Springs, you might be able to tap into your remaining entitlement to assume another VA loan.

## How to Check Your VA Eligibility

Don't guess. Go to VA.gov and pull your Certificate of Eligibility (COE).

You can request it online in like five minutes. Upload your discharge papers (DD-214), and the VA will email you your COE. Print that thing out. You're going to need it.

Your Certificate of Eligibility shows your entitlement amount. In 2025, that's up to about $645,000 in guaranty. That number matters when you're looking at assumptions because the seller's loan balance has to fit within what's left of your entitlement.

Confused? Here's an example. Say you used $400,000 of your VA entitlement on a previous VA loan that you paid off. You have $245,000 left to work with. If the house you want to assume has a $350,000 VA loan on it, you can't assume that loan without the seller coming back and having their entitlement restored. (More on that in a second.)

## The Seller's Side: This is Important

Here's where most people get confused.

When you assume a VA loan, the seller doesn't lose their VA benefit automatically. But they stay on the hook with the VA until your loan is paid off or you pay cash to close it out completely. That's a big deal for them.

So sellers usually want the buyer (you) to be eligible for VA loans. Why? Because if you're VA-eligible and you assume their VA loan, their entitlement gets restored. They can go buy another house with their own VA loan. They're free.

If you assume their VA loan and you're not VA-eligible, they stay liable with the VA forever. Not ideal. The VA can come after them if you default.

This is why a lot of VA loans don't get assumed. The seller's realtor doesn't know about it, or the seller is worried about their VA status. But once you explain it to them, it makes total sense. Their entitlement comes back. Everyone wins.

## What the Lender Checks

When you apply to assume a VA loan, the lender is going to verify:
1. Your VA eligibility (your COE)
2. Your credit score (usually 620 minimum, but most lenders want 640 or higher)
3. Your debt-to-income ratio (they want to see you can handle the payment plus your other debts)
4. Your employment history (typically last two years)
5. Your residual income (basically, after taxes, housing, and debts, do you have enough money left over each month)

That last one is important. The VA cares that you're not stretched too thin. If the house is expensive and your income doesn't support it, they'll deny the assumption. It's not just about credit.

## Down Payment: This is Where It Gets Good

Here's the thing about VA loan assumptions that blows people's minds.

You don't need to put 20% down. You don't need to put 10% down. You can assume a VA loan with zero down payment. Seriously.

The only thing you have to cover is the difference between the sale price and the loan balance. And sometimes the seller will cover part of that to make the deal work. Especially if they understand that assuming the loan brings their entitlement back.

I've seen Colorado Springs military families assume VA loans with $3,000 down on $450,000 houses. That's wild compared to a traditional mortgage where you need 5% to 20%.

This is why I keep saying assumptions are a different animal. The rate is locked in. The payment is low. And the down payment can be minimal.

## Occupancy: You Have to Live There

The VA requires that you occupy the house as your primary residence within 60 days of closing. If you're buying an investment property or a second home, VA loans don't work. This is a rule for the original buyer and for you as the assumor.

If you assume a VA loan and don't move in, the VA can call the loan due. Don't do that.

## Timeline and Process

Once you're approved to assume, the paperwork takes about 30 to 45 days. The lender will order the VA appraisal, check your eligibility, and process everything. It's slower than a regular purchase, but it's doable.

The seller needs to cooperate with the lender and provide documentation about the current loan. Most sellers are fine with this once they understand their entitlement comes back.

## Here's the Thing

VA loan assumptions are underused in Colorado Springs. A lot of military families don't even know they can assume an existing VA loan, and a lot of sellers don't know how it works. When both sides understand the rules, it's a win for everyone.

You get a locked-in rate. You put down minimal cash. The seller gets their entitlement restored. Everyone walks away happy.
