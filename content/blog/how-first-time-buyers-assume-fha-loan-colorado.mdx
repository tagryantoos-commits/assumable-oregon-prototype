# How First-Time Buyers Can Assume an FHA Loan in Colorado

Here's the thing about being a first-time buyer right now. You're staring at mortgage rates around 6.5% to 7%. You're doing the math on a $300K house and realizing your payment is $2,000 plus. And you're wondering if you should just wait for rates to drop.

But there's another option sitting right in front of you.

FHA assumable loans. And honestly, I see first-time buyers sleep on this all the time.

An assumable mortgage means you're taking over someone else's loan. You inherit their rate. Their term. Their balance. And for first-time buyers, especially in Colorado, this can mean the difference between affording a home and renting for another two years.

Let me walk you through how this actually works.

## What You're Actually Assuming

When you assume an FHA loan, you're stepping into someone else's mortgage. That person had a rate locked in (maybe 3.5%, maybe 4.2%, doesn't matter). You take that exact rate. You don't get a new one. The bank doesn't renegotiate with you.

So if the seller bought in 2021 at 2.75%, you get 2.75%. No negotiation. No "but rates are higher now." You get what they got.

Why would someone let you do this? Because they're selling the house. They're moving on. And your assumption lets them sell faster because you're not waiting on a traditional mortgage approval that takes 30 days and falls apart half the time.

## The Money Part (This is the Real Story)

Let's say you're looking at a $350,000 house in Colorado Springs. Assumable loan. 3.2% rate. 23 years left on the note. Balance is $285,000.

Your payment: $1,287.

Now compare that to buying the same house with a new mortgage at today's rates (let's say 6.8%). You'd put 5% down ($17,500). You're financing $332,500. Your payment: $2,145.

That's $858 more every single month. That's $10,296 a year. Over the life of a 30-year loan, that's not just more money. That's $309,000 more in payments.

For a first-time buyer, that difference might mean you don't qualify for the loan. Your debt-to-income ratio is too high. Your lender says no. Game over.

With the assumption, you're actually in the conversation.

## Here's What Lenders Want to See

This is where a lot of first-time buyers get nervous. They think an assumption is some kind of loophole. It's not. Lenders still want to approve you. They just want different things than a traditional mortgage does.

**Income and debt ratio.** The lender is running your credit and your income against the loan payment. So if you're assuming a $285K loan at $1,287 a month, they need to see you can actually afford that. No different than a regular mortgage, just a lower payment.

**Credit score.** FHA assumable loans typically want a 580 minimum, but 620 is more realistic. If you're a first-time buyer, you might not have a ton of credit history. That's okay. The lender gets it. What they want is no major red flags. No foreclosures in the last three years. No Chapter 7 bankruptcies in the last two years.

**Down payment.** Here's where first-time buyers catch a break. FHA assumable loans let you put down as little as 3.5%. On a $350K house with an assumable balance of $285K, you need $10,000 down (plus closing costs, which run another $3,500 to $5,000). That's workable for a first-time buyer.

**The appraisal.** The lender orders an appraisal. They need to make sure the house is actually worth what you're paying. This is the gatekeeper. If the house appraises low, the deal gets weird. But that's not unique to assumptions. That happens on every deal.

## The Paperwork and Timeline

Okay. Real talk. Assumptions aren't fast. They're not like buying on your own. You're not just signing loan docs and done.

First, the lender has to approve you. That takes 7 to 10 days if you're organized and they're moving. Then the assumption paperwork goes to the servicer (the company that manages the loan). The servicer has to approve the assumption. That's another 5 to 10 days. Then you close.

Total timeline: 15 to 25 days, usually. That's longer than a traditional mortgage when things go right. But shorter when traditional deals have issues.

The paperwork itself isn't complicated, but there's more of it. You fill out forms for the lender. The lender sends docs to the servicer. The servicer sends back final approval. You sign the assumption agreement. You wire money. Done.

## Why First-Time Buyers Should Care Right Now

If you're a first-time buyer in Colorado, the math right now is brutal. Rates are high. Down payments feel impossible. Lenders are strict. Monthly payments are crazy.

An assumable loan fixes three of those four problems. You get a lower rate (way lower). Your payment is smaller (hundreds a month lower). Your down payment can be tiny (3.5%). The lender approval is still real, but less painful because you're not asking them to take on as much risk.

I'm not saying every assumable loan works. Some sellers don't have assumable mortgages. Some do but they're not motivated to let someone assume (they want all cash). Some have rates so high they're not worth assuming.

But when the stars align, this is a first-time buyer's secret weapon.

## Where to Start

First, figure out what you can actually afford. What's your down payment fund look like? What's your debt-to-income ratio? What's your credit score? Get real about that before you waste time.

Then, start looking at assumable loans specifically. Not every house for sale has one. But they're out there. More than you think.

Next, talk to a lender who actually does assumptions. Not every mortgage company does them. Some avoid them because they're not the traditional path. Find one that knows what they're doing. They'll tell you what they need and how fast they can move.
