Alright. If you're military and looking to buy in Colorado Springs right now, you've got a choice. And I'm going to give you the numbers so you can actually decide instead of just guessing.

New VA loan or VA assumption. Let's figure out which one actually saves you money.

## The Setup: What We're Comparing

First, context. A new VA loan is what you probably think of when you think "VA benefit." You go to a bank, you qualify, you get a new mortgage with current market rates.

A VA assumption is different. Someone else bought their house with a VA loan (probably years ago when rates were lower). You take over that mortgage instead of getting a new one. You inherit their rate, their payment, basically their whole loan. The original borrower stays on the hook with the VA, but the lender releases them.

Not a ton of people know this exists. But it's there. And for Colorado Springs right now, the math gets interesting real fast.

## The Real Numbers: Today's Market

Let's say you're buying a $425,000 house in Colorado Springs. That's close to the current market average out here.

**Scenario 1: New VA Loan**

Current VA rates (early 2025) are sitting around 5.5% to 6%. Let's use 5.75% because that's realistic for someone with solid credit.

$425,000 purchase price. No down payment (because VA loans don't require one). 

Your payment: $2,479 per month (principal and interest only).

That's $29,748 a year just in P&I.

**Scenario 2: VA Assumption**

Let's say this house was originally bought in 2021. The seller locked in a 2.85% VA rate (which was common then).

Same $425,000 purchase price. Same house. Different loan.

Your payment: $1,687 per month.

That's $20,244 a year.

The difference: **$792 a month. $9,504 a year.**

Over a 30-year loan, assuming rates stay where they are and you don't refinance, you're looking at roughly **$285,000 in savings** just from the lower rate.

Let me say that again. Two hundred and eighty-five thousand dollars.

## But Wait. There's More to It.

I know what you're thinking. Too good to be true, right.

There's friction. Assumptions aren't just "sign here and you're done."

**The VA has to approve it.** The bank has to approve it. The seller has to cooperate. You have to actually qualify for the assumption (your income, your credit, your debt-to-income ratio all matter). 

Currently, non-veteran assumptions are getting approved at about a 20% success rate in Colorado. Veterans are closer to 60-70%. So if you're a vet, your odds are better. But it's not a slam dunk.

The process also takes longer. A new VA loan takes 30-40 days typically. An assumption can take 60-90 days because of the extra approval layer. That matters if you're on a timeline.

And here's the thing that surprises people: **You might need to bring cash to closing.**

If the house is worth more than the loan balance (which it probably is), you have to cover that gap. Let's say the remaining loan balance is $350,000 but the house is worth $425,000. That's a $75,000 difference. You need to bring that cash to close.

That's real money out of your pocket.

With a new VA loan, you can pay less down (or nothing). More flexible.

## The Qualification Piece

Here's another thing. VA assumptions assume you have liquid capital available. Banks want to see reserves. They want to see that you can actually handle the down payment portion.

A lot of military families are living paycheck to paycheck. Nothing wrong with that. But it means an assumption might not be possible even if the rate is incredible.

New VA loans are easier to qualify for if you've got limited cash on hand because there's no down payment required.

## So Which One Saves You More Money.

If you qualify for an assumption on a house with a killer rate locked in? The assumption wins. By a lot.

If the seller's rate is only 4% or higher, the gap shrinks. It's still usually in the assumption's favor, but the spread gets tighter.

If you don't have $50-100K in cash available for a down payment? New VA loan is more realistic.

If you're in a hot market and need to close in 30 days? New VA loan is faster.

**But if the rate is good. If you have the cash. If you have time. The assumption is the move.**

I've seen Colorado Springs families put an extra $200-300K in their pocket over the life of the loan by assuming instead of taking new.

## The Strategy Layer

Here's what a lot of military buyers miss. You don't have to pick one blind.

Get pre-approved for a new VA loan. That takes a few days. Know your backup.

Then shop for assumable properties. Your real estate agent should be pulling comparable sales with assumable loans baked in. Not all agents do this. Ours does because it's literally what we specialize in.

If you find an assumable property with a rate below 4%, run the numbers. Calculate the down payment you'd need. See if it pencils out better than a new loan.

Then decide.

You might find nothing. The market might not have what you need at a price that makes sense.

Or you might find something that saves you a quarter million dollars.

Honestly, the people who aren't even looking are costing themselves money.

## One More Consideration: Rate Risk

If you take a new VA loan today at 5.75%, that's your rate. It's locked. Done.

If rates drop to 4.5% in two years, you can refinance and get that new rate (it'll cost you money upfront, but you can do it).

If you assume at 2.85% and rates stay where they are? You're golden. You got the deal.

But you're also locked in. You can't refinance down because you already have the best rate in the room. You can only refinance up if rates go higher (which would be weird).

This is why an assumption works best in a higher-rate environment. Which, spoiler alert, is where we are right now.
