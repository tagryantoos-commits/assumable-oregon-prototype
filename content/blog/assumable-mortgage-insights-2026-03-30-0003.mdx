Alright, so here's the thing. We're in this weird moment right now where everything people thought they knew about buying a house is kind of. not applying anymore.

Rates are bouncing around. Inventory is still tight. And sellers are realizing their 2.5% mortgage from 2021 is worth actual money. Like, real money.

That's where assumable mortgages come in. And 2026 is shaping up to be the year more people figure this out.

Let me walk you through what's happening and why you should care.

## The Rate Situation is Messy

First, the obvious part. If you're shopping for a mortgage today, you're probably looking at something between 6% and 7%. That sucks compared to what people locked in a few years ago. But that's the market.

Now picture this. A house on the market has a 2.65% assumable mortgage sitting underneath it. The seller's payment is $1,083 a month on a $380K loan.

You come in. You assume that mortgage. Your payment for the exact same debt? Still $1,083 a month. Your rate? Still 2.65%.

That's not normal. That doesn't happen in regular real estate deals. And it's worth understanding why.

## What Makes This Different Right Now

Assumable mortgages aren't new. But the gap between old rates and new rates makes them valuable in a way they haven't been in about 15 years.

When rates are close together (like if you're choosing between 6.5% and 6.8%), an assumable mortgage doesn't move the needle. You save maybe $50 a month. Not worth the extra paperwork.

But when the spread is 3-4 percentage points. That's when people start asking questions.

Here's real math. Let's say you're looking at a $400K loan.

At 6.5% over 30 years, you're paying $2,527 a month.

Same loan at 2.65% (assuming it). You're paying $1,649 a month.

That's $878 a month. $10,536 a year. Over 10 years, you're looking at over $100K in savings before you even factor in principal paydown differences.

That changes the conversation. That changes what house you can afford. That changes whether you can actually build wealth instead of just paying the bank.

## Why Sellers Are Suddenly Interested

For years, assumable mortgages were invisible. Sellers didn't mention them. Agents didn't market them. Buyers didn't know they existed. Check out our guide on [how to assume an FHA mortgage](/blog/how-to-assume-fha-mortgage-step-by-step) if you want the technical breakdown.

But right now, sellers are in a tough spot. They've got a 2.5% rate. You don't. So the house sits. Or it sits at a price point that doesn't work for anyone.

Smart sellers realized something. If they lead with the assumable mortgage in the listing, they're offering buyers something no other house on the block can offer. A lower payment. A lower rate. A legitimate financial advantage.

It's becoming a selling point. Not because the seller is being nice. Because it actually moves inventory.

## The Catch (There's Always a Catch)

I want to be super clear about this part because a lot of people don't understand it.

You still need to qualify. The lender is going to underwrite you. They're going to look at your debt-to-income ratio, your credit, your income, everything. Just like a regular mortgage.

The difference is you're not refinancing. You're not shopping for a new rate. You're taking over an existing loan. The approval process is usually faster, but it's not a shortcut.

Also, the seller will want you to bring something to closing. Maybe not a full down payment, but something. If the house is worth $500K and the loan balance is $380K, you're covering that $120K gap somehow. Cash, another loan, seller credit, trade equity. Something.

Most assumable mortgages right now are FHA loans. Some are VA. Very few are conventional. If you want the nitty-gritty on what you're actually looking at, I've got a deeper dive [here on assumable mortgages in Colorado Springs](/blog/assumable-mortgages-in-colorado-springs).

## Why People Are Sleeping on This

Three reasons.

One: Real estate agents in a lot of markets don't deal with these often enough to be comfortable explaining them. So they don't. Easier to ignore than to educate.

Two: The mortgage industry benefits from you getting a new loan. They don't benefit from you assuming an old one. So nobody's advertising this to you.

Three: There's real work involved. You need a lender who understands assumptions. You need a title company that knows what they're doing. You need an agent who actually knows this space. We handle this [all the time](/blog/assumable-mortgage-low-down-payment-colorado), but a lot of markets? It's rare enough to be weird.

## What This Means for You in 2026

If rates stay elevated (and honestly, who knows), assumable mortgages become more attractive every month. The longer current rates sit, the more valuable that 2.5% or 2.65% becomes.

Inventory is still tight. But houses with assumable mortgages can move. They're a tool that actually works right now.

If you're buying, you should absolutely be asking about this. "Does this property have an assumable mortgage?" should be one of your first questions.

If you're selling, and you've got a low rate, this is your leverage. This is how you compete against newly built homes and other inventory.
