# How FHA Loan Assumptions Work in Colorado Springs (Step-by-Step)

Alright. Let's talk about FHA loan assumptions. This is probably the easiest assumable mortgage to understand, which is why I want to walk you through exactly how it works.

First things first. If you're buying a house in Colorado Springs with an FHA loan on it from before 1986, you're basically getting a free pass. Seriously. No approval needed. You just take it over. But if that loan was originated after 1986 (spoiler: most of them were), you'll need HUD approval. And that's what we're going to break down today.

Here's why this matters. Right now, if you're shopping for a new mortgage, you're looking at 6.5% to 7% rates. But assume an FHA loan from 2021 or 2022? You could lock in 2.75% to 3.5%. That's the difference between a $1,200 payment and a $1,800 payment on a $400K home. That's $7,200 a year. Multiply that over 30 years and you're talking about real money.

## Step 1. Find the Right Property with an Assumable FHA Loan

This is the foundation. Not every house has an assumable FHA mortgage. You need a seller who has an FHA loan on the property. 

When you're looking at listings in Colorado Springs, you need to know what loan the seller has. That information should be in the listing or you can ask your agent (or me). If it's FHA, and it was originated after 1986, you can assume it. If it's conventional or VA, different rules apply.

The sweet spot is finding an FHA loan with a rate locked in before 2023. Rates dropped hard in 2020 and 2021. If you can grab one at 3% or lower, you're looking at serious long-term savings compared to what's available today.

## Step 2. Get Pre-Approval from the Lender (and HUD Approval)

Here's where it gets real. The current lender (usually a bank) has to agree to the assumption. They're not going to just hand over a 3% mortgage to anyone who asks.

You'll need to:

1. Submit a formal application to assume the loan.
2. Provide proof of income, credit report, and employment verification.
3. Go through underwriting. Yes, a full underwriting process, just like a regular mortgage.

HUD is also involved. They're going to look at your credit and financials and decide if you're a safe bet to take over that loan. 

This typically takes 30 to 45 days. Sometimes longer. It's not fast, but it's worth it.

**Here's the honest part.** If you have bad credit or a weak financial profile, they can deny you. The lender isn't trying to make this easy. They'd rather have you get a new loan at 7% because that's more profitable for them. But if your finances are solid, you'll get approved.

## Step 3. Negotiate the Price and Down Payment

This is where your agent (cough, like me) earns their money.

When you assume a loan, you're taking over what the seller owes. Let's say the seller bought the house for $450K and they owe $380K on that 2.8% FHA mortgage. If the house is worth $500K today, you need to cover the gap.

That gap is negotiable.

You could offer $500K and put down $120K ($500K minus $380K assumed). Or you could negotiate the price down to $480K because the seller is getting the benefit of a quick, assumable mortgage deal. Then your down payment would be $100K.

Some sellers will price lower if they know they're getting out faster and cleaner.

Down payment minimums for FHA assumptions are typically 3% to 5% of the purchase price. But there's flexibility here depending on what you and the seller agree on.

## Step 4. Title Search and Property Appraisal

The lender is going to want an appraisal. They need to know the property is worth what you're paying for it. If it appraises low, they might not approve the assumption. So make sure the numbers make sense.

A title search protects you. It confirms there are no liens or ownership issues that could blow up the deal.

## Step 5. The Closing Process

This is where everything comes together.

Your title company will prepare the assumption documents. You'll sign paperwork transferring the note and mortgage to your name. The seller will sign off. Funds will transfer. And boom. That FHA loan is now yours.

Closing costs on an assumption are typically lower than a new mortgage. You're not originating a new loan. You're transferring an existing one. Expect $2,000 to $5,000 in closing costs, not $8,000 to $15,000 like a traditional purchase.

## Why This Matters in Colorado Springs Right Now

Colorado Springs is a competitive market. Inventory is limited. Rates are high. An assumable FHA mortgage at 3% versus a new mortgage at 6.8% is not a small thing. We're talking about $300 to $400 a month in payment difference on a typical home.

That's $3,600 to $4,800 a year.

Over the life of the loan, if you assume at 3% instead of getting a new mortgage at 6.8%, you could save $150,000 to $200,000.

If that doesn't get your attention, I don't know what will.

## What Can Go Wrong

I want to be straight with you. This process isn't bulletproof.

The lender can deny your application. They can say no to the assumption, and you're back to square one. Your credit, your employment, your debt-to-income ratio. All of it matters.

The appraisal could come in low. Suddenly the numbers don't work.

The seller could back out if they're not happy with the price negotiations.

An assumption can also fall apart if you can't close within the timeframe the lender sets. Some lenders give you 30 to 45 days. If you miss that window, the deal is done.

That's why it's critical to work with people who know what they're doing. This isn't a standard real estate transaction. It requires attention to detail.

## The Bottom Line

FHA loan assumptions are one of the most underutilized tools in real estate. Most people don't even know they exist. The ones who do are quietly locking in rates that would normally take a time machine to access.

In Colorado Springs, with inventory tight and rates high, an assumable FHA mortgage can be the difference between a deal that makes sense and one that doesn't.

If you're looking at a house with an assumable FHA loan on it, don't gloss over that detail. Run the numbers. Compare your payment on the assumption versus a new mortgage. The difference might surprise you.
