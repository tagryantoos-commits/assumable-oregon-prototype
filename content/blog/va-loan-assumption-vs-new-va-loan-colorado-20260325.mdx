# VA Loan Assumption vs. New VA Loan in Colorado: Which Is Better in 2025?

Alright, here's the thing. If you're a veteran shopping for a home in Colorado right now, you've got a decision that literally comes down to thousands of dollars over the life of your loan. And honestly, most vets don't even know they have the choice.

New VA loans are great. Everyone knows about those. But VA loan assumptions? That's the move that's changing the game for a lot of people who know about it.

Let me walk you through the actual numbers so you can see which one makes sense for you.

## The Setup: What We're Actually Comparing

A new VA loan in 2025 is running somewhere around 6.5% to 7% depending on the day and your credit. Down payment is zero. You're getting a fresh start with a new lender.

A VA loan assumption means you're taking over someone else's existing mortgage. That person locked in their rate years ago. Maybe it was 3%, maybe 4%, maybe even lower. You step into that loan, take over their payments, and boom. You're borrowing at their historical rate.

This is where the magic happens.

## The Money Part: Let's Use Real Numbers

Say you're looking at a $500,000 home in Colorado Springs.

**New VA Loan scenario:**
- Rate: 6.75% (current market)
- Loan amount: $500,000
- Monthly payment: $3,310
- Total interest over 30 years: $691,000

**VA Assumption scenario (assuming 3.5% rate from seller):**
- Rate: 3.5% (existing mortgage)
- Loan amount: $500,000
- Monthly payment: $2,245
- Total interest over 30 years: $308,000

The difference? You're saving $1,065 every single month. That's $12,780 a year. Over 30 years, you save $383,000 in interest alone.

Like, that's not small money. That's a down payment on another house. That's retirement savings. That's your kids' college fund.

## But Wait. There's Paperwork.

Here's the thing I need to be super clear about: VA assumptions aren't harder than new VA loans. They're just different. And they require the seller to cooperate.

The seller has to agree to it. The lender has to approve the assumption (this isn't guaranteed). You need to qualify based on income and credit, same as a new loan. And yeah, there's paperwork. More paperwork than a standard new purchase sometimes.

But is that trade-off worth saving $383,000? For most people, absolutely yes.

## What About That Seller's Interest Rate?

This is the question everyone asks: "How does the seller get such a low rate?"

They locked it in when rates were actually reasonable. Maybe 2018. Maybe 2019. Maybe even early 2020. A lot of Colorado homeowners sitting in 2.5% to 4% mortgages right now.

Those rates exist. They're out there. The sellers have them. And if you can assume their loan, you get access to those rates too.

That's the whole concept.

## VA Assumptions vs. New VA Loans: The Real Comparison

Let me break this down into what actually matters to you:

**New VA Loan:**
- Pro: No cooperation needed from seller. You're the primary borrower. You control the timeline.
- Pro: Full 30-year amortization. You know exactly when you're paid off.
- Con: You're paying 6.5% to 7% right now. That's the market.
- Con: You'll pay 300K plus in interest on a $500K loan.

**VA Assumption:**
- Pro: Locked-in rate from years ago. Could be 2% to 4%.
- Pro: Save $200K to $400K in interest over the loan.
- Pro: Lower monthly payment means better cash flow and easier qualification.
- Con: Seller has to agree and lender has to approve.
- Con: Remaining loan term might be 20 years instead of 30 (depends on when the original seller got the mortgage).
- Con: If the original loan was a 15-year mortgage, you're inheriting that shorter timeline.

## The Assumption Game Changer: Remaining Loan Term

Here's what nobody talks about. If someone got their VA loan in 2015 for 30 years, we're now 10 years into it. That means you're assuming a 20-year loan, not a 30-year loan.

This matters because it changes your monthly payment math.

**Example:**
- $500,000 loan at 3.5% with 20 years remaining.
- Monthly payment: $2,767
- That's still $540 cheaper than the new loan.

Even with a shorter timeline, you're usually winning on the rate.

## Who Should Assume?

You should assume if:
- You find a property with a low-rate VA loan the seller will let you take over.
- You qualify based on income (your debt-to-income ratio needs to look good).
- You can handle the slightly shorter loan term.
- You understand the risks and the approval process.

You should get a new VA loan if:
- There's no assumable loan available on the property you want.
- The seller won't cooperate with an assumption.
- You want to restart the 30-year clock.
- You prefer the simplicity and control of starting fresh.

Both are legitimate. But the math usually favors assumptions if they're available.

## The Hard Truth: Not Every Assumption Works

I want to be honest. Not every VA assumption closes. Lenders can pull the plug. If your credit comes back weird or your income doesn't support the loan, they'll deny you.

The approval rate for non-veterans on assumptions is around 20%. For veterans assuming a VA loan, it's higher. But it's not guaranteed.

And sometimes the seller backs out. Or the appraisal comes in low. Or the lender decides they don't want to do the assumption.

This is why you need a team that knows these loans inside out. You need someone who's done 500 of them, not someone doing their first one.

## What If You Can't Assume?

If the VA assumption doesn't work out, a new VA loan is sitting right there as your backup plan. No penalty for trying. You just move forward with a standard VA purchase, get that 6.75% rate, and build your Colorado home.

Both paths get you to homeownership. One just costs way less if it comes through.
