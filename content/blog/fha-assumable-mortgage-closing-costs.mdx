# FHA Assumable Mortgage Closing Costs: What to Expect

Alright, so you're looking at an assumable FHA mortgage and you want to know the real cost to close. Not the glossy version. The actual number you're writing a check for.

Here's the thing. FHA closing costs on an assumption are way lower than a traditional purchase. But there are still costs. And I want you to know exactly what they are before you're sitting across from a closing attorney wondering why the bill is what it is.

Let me walk you through this.

## The Short Answer

You're looking at somewhere between $2,500 and $6,000 in total closing costs on an FHA assumption. Could be less if you're in a low-cost market. Could be a bit more in high-priced areas. But that's the real range.

Compare that to a standard mortgage closing where you're paying 2-5% of the loan amount. On a $350,000 loan, that's $7,000 to $17,500. So yeah. You're saving big here.

## What Actually Makes Up That $2,500 to $6,000

Let me break down the line items you'll see on your Closing Disclosure.

### FHA Assumption Fee ($900 to $1,200)

This one's non-negotiable. HUD charges a fee to process your assumption request. It's their way of saying "okay, we reviewed your paperwork and we're transferring this loan to your name."

The exact amount depends on the loan balance and whether you're assuming the existing FHA mortgage insurance or getting a new policy. Most of the time, you're keeping the existing insurance (which is part of why this whole thing is so much cheaper than a new loan).

### Title Search and Title Insurance ($500 to $1,500)

You need to know the property has a clean title. No liens hiding in the background. No ex-spouse claiming ownership. The title company digs into the records and confirms you're actually getting what you think you're getting.

Title insurance then protects you if someone pops up later claiming they have rights to the property. It's not optional. Lenders require it.

The cost varies wildly by state and county. Colorado Springs is different from Louisville, which is different from Savannah. Gives you a real sense of regional differences.

### Appraisal ($400 to $700)

The lender needs to know the property's worth. This protects them (and you). An appraiser comes out, walks the property, and gives a professional opinion on value.

Here's the thing. Sometimes the seller's loan servicer waives this if the loan is recent and the home hasn't changed much. Not guaranteed though. Budget for it.

### Attorney Fees and Closing Fees ($600 to $1,200)

Someone's gotta coordinate the closing. Review documents. Handle the paperwork with the county. Transfer the deed. That's your closing attorney or title company's job.

Colorado Springs closing attorneys typically charge on the lower end. Other markets are pricier. It depends on how much work is involved and what your local market rates are.

### Home Inspection (Optional, but Smart: $300 to $500)

I'm gonna be real with you. This isn't a closing cost per se. You don't pay it at closing. But you pay it before you go under contract.

Get an inspection. Seriously. An assumable mortgage still means you're buying the house. You need to know if the roof is about to crater or if the foundation's cracking.

### Credit Report ($75 to $150)

The lender pulls your credit to qualify you. It's a small line on the Closing Disclosure but it's there.

### Recording Fees ($100 to $300)

The county records the deed transfer. They charge a fee based on the document size and your local recording rates. It's usually cheap but depends on where you are.

## What You're NOT Paying

And this is the part that makes assumptions so good.

You're not paying loan origination fees. The lender isn't originating a new loan. You're stepping into an existing one.

You're not paying points.

You're not paying underwriting fees.

You're not paying appraisal management company fees or processing fees or rate lock fees.

You're keeping the existing FHA mortgage insurance. You're not getting a new insurance policy, which saves you thousands on day one. (On a $350,000 loan, new FHA insurance runs around $10,000 to $15,000. You're not paying that.)

This is why the assumption is so brutally good for your wallet.

## Real World Example

Let's say you're assuming a $350,000 FHA loan in Colorado Springs.

FHA assumption fee: $1,100
Title search and insurance: $900
Appraisal: $550
Attorney and closing: $1,000
Credit report: $100
Recording: $200

Total closing costs: $3,850

Now compare that to getting a brand new mortgage for $350,000 at conventional rates.

Closing costs on a new loan: $7,000 to $12,000 (usually 2-3% of the loan amount)

You just saved $3,000 to $8,000 before you even talk about the rate difference.

If you're assuming at 3.2% instead of getting a new loan at 6.5%, you're saving $180,000 in interest over 30 years. That's not hyperbole. That's just math.

## Who Pays What

Here's where it gets a little weird sometimes.

You (the buyer) are typically paying all of these costs. The title insurance, appraisal, attorney fees, recording. It all goes on your tab at closing.

The seller isn't typically paying any of it. They're not financing anything or kicking in for your assumption fees. Their incentive is that they sell the house. That's it.

This is different from a traditional sale where a seller might contribute to buyer closing costs. On an assumption, you're not negotiating that.

You can always try to ask the seller to contribute something. But they've got no obligation to. And honestly, most sellers don't because they don't fully understand the process.
