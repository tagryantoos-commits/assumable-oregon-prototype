Alright, so we're almost three months into 2026 and things are shifting. I'm seeing patterns in what people are asking, what deals are moving, and what's actually working right now. Let me walk you through what I'm seeing on the ground.

## The Rate Environment Changed, Again

Here's the thing about 2026. Everyone expected rates to stay in that 4.5 to 5% range through the first quarter. That didn't happen. We've had two rate cuts and a pause. Volatility is back.

This matters for assumable mortgages because the math changed. A lot.

Three months ago, assuming a 2.85% loan felt like a no-brainer. The gap between that rate and what you'd get new was huge. Like, $200 a month huge on a $400K loan. Now some sellers are actually seeing current rates come closer to their assumed rate. Not all the way down, but closer.

So what does that mean for buyers right now? It means you have to do your homework. You can't just assume a loan and think you're winning. You need to know exactly what rate you'd get conventionally, then subtract the assumption cost (the bump in principal, appraisal, title work, legal fees). We're talking $3K to $8K depending on the loan.

Sometimes the math still crushes it. Sometimes it's closer.

## Sellers Are Finally Getting It

Here's what's wild. Sellers used to think their assumable mortgage was a throwaway feature. Like, "Oh, it's assumable, cool, whatever." Now they're actively asking about it. They're looking at comps that sold with assumptions and realizing they can market differently.

I had a conversation with a seller last week who said, "Can we price this higher because the buyer gets a low rate?" The answer is yes and no. Yes, it's valuable. No, don't get weird about it. The market's not stupid. A buyer will do the math. But smart pricing that shows the value of assumption usually means the property moves faster and for closer to list.

The sellers winning right now are the ones treating the assumable mortgage as part of the story, not a surprise bonus.

## Buyer Psychology Is Shifting

People are tired of shopping rates. I know that sounds simple, but it matters.

Conventional mortgage shopping in 2026 involves calling three lenders, comparing closing costs, dealing with rate locks expiring. It's a headache. Assuming a mortgage? You know the rate upfront. No surprises. No "lock expires in 10 days" stress. The paperwork is different (and yes, more of it), but psychologically people like knowing what they're getting.

Younger buyers especially are gravitating toward assumptions now. Gen Z and millennial buyers see it as a way to make a down payment go further. Instead of needing $80K down on a conventional deal, maybe you get in with $40K on an assumable. That math is hitting different right now.

## What's Not Working Anymore

OK, so what should you know about deals that are harder to move in early 2026?

Assumption deals on properties that need work. If the house needs new roof, foundation issues, or significant updates, the buyer is going to max out their cash on assuming the loan and then have nothing left for repairs. That property sits. The seller assumes they need a buyer with cash. They don't. They need a property that's move-in ready or close to it.

Also, assumption deals above $700K are slower. Not impossible, but slower. The jumbo assumption market is thin. Buyers at that price point are usually more rate-conscious (they're paying more anyway) and they want conventional financing with more lender options.

## The Appraisal Thing Is Real Now

In early 2026, lenders are being stricter on appraisals for assumptions. They want to make sure the equity is actually there. I had one deal last month where the seller expected the home to appraise at $475K. It came in at $451K. The buyer couldn't assume the full loan balance because the lender said no.

Appraisal gaps on assumptions are becoming the new negotiation point. Sellers who price realistically upfront don't hit this problem. Sellers who overprice are stuck.

## Here's What's Working

So if you're thinking about assuming or selling with an assumption, here's what I'm seeing move.

One: properties that are assumable with a rate between 2.5% and 3.5% in this market. The gap between that and current rates is still significant enough that buyers will handle the paperwork. We're talking $150 to $250 a month in savings on a $400K loan over 30 years. That's real money.

Two: sellers who position the assumption as a primary marketing tool, not an afterthought. List it in the headline. Talk about the payment. Show the buyer what they're getting.

Three: properties in Colorado Springs, Thornton, and surrounding areas where we have a proven process. If you're trying to assume a loan in a market where lenders are unfamiliar with the process, it gets messy.

## What I'd Do Right Now

If you're a buyer, get your financials together and find an assumable that pencils out. Don't assume just to assume. Run the numbers. Compare the assumed payment plus the assumption costs versus a conventional loan at current rates. If you save money and the property is right, move.

If you're a seller, be honest about what you have. A 3% assumable mortgage is an asset. Price accordingly and market it as one.
