# VA Loan Assumption Eligibility: Who Qualifies and How It Works in Colorado

Here's the thing about VA loans. They're some of the best mortgages out there. Low rates, no down payment required, built-in buyer protections. But here's what most people don't realize: if a VA loan is locked in at 2.5% and rates are sitting at 6% or 7%, that mortgage becomes valuable in a completely different way.

That's where assumption comes in.

I get this question all the time in Colorado. "Ryan, can I assume a VA loan?" And the answer depends on who you are. Let me walk you through exactly how this works and who actually qualifies.

## The Basic Rule: Who Can Assume a VA Loan

The VA allows two types of people to assume a VA mortgage:

**1. Another veteran (with available entitlement)**
**2. A non-veteran (with some real restrictions)**

That's it. Those are your lanes. Let's break them down.

### Veterans Assuming VA Loans

If you're a veteran and you want to assume someone else's VA loan in Colorado, here's what you need:

Available VA entitlement. Your entitlement is basically your VA borrowing power. Every veteran gets a standard amount. If you've used it on a previous VA loan, you might not have any left. Or you might have some left to use.

Here's how it works. Say you got a VA loan for $300K back in and you've paid it down to $250K. You used $300K of your entitlement. If your total entitlement is $636K (current max), you've got $336K of entitlement still available. That's what you use for the assumption.

The veteran seller's lender has to approve the assumption. This is huge. Just because you have entitlement doesn't mean the bank says yes. They'll run your credit, check your income, verify your service, and make sure you can actually afford the payment.

In Colorado right now, we're seeing most VA loan assumptions approved for veterans. Credit score needs to be solid (usually 620 minimum, but stronger is better). Debt-to-income ratio matters. Can't be overleveraged.

The VA doesn't charge an assumption fee. The seller might charge some costs. There are closing costs involved. But the VA side stays clean.

### Non-Veterans Assuming VA Loans

This is where it gets tricky, and honestly, this is where most people get confused or just give up.

A non-veteran can assume a VA loan. But there's a catch that stops most deals cold.

The original veteran's entitlement stays tied up in the loan. Forever. Until the loan is paid off or the property is sold. So when a non-veteran assumes the VA loan, that veteran doesn't get their entitlement back. They can't use it again.

Most veterans don't want to do that. Their entitlement is valuable to them. So they resist the assumption. Or they demand the non-veteran buyer pay them extra to compensate.

But here's the real blocker: the VA requires the non-veteran to meet the same approval standards as anyone getting a new mortgage. Credit check, income verification, debt-to-income ratio. All of it. Except one thing is different.

The non-veteran has to put down a down payment. The VA loan itself doesn't require one, but the lender will usually ask for 3% to 5% down to offset their perceived risk. Sometimes more.

So here's the math. You're looking at a $400K Colorado home with an assumable VA loan at 2.8%. As a non-veteran:

You're putting down $12K to $20K out of pocket. Your payment goes from maybe $1,330 to $1,410 depending on taxes and insurance (still massively cheaper than getting a new loan at today's rates). You're subject to full underwriting. The timeline is longer.

But you're getting a 2.8% rate when the market is offering 6%.

For the right buyer, that's worth it.

## How the Approval Process Actually Works in Colorado

Alright, so you're interested. You found a Colorado property with a VA loan you want to assume. Here's the step-by-step:

**Step 1: Get the loan details from the seller's agent.** Loan number, lender, current rate, current balance, remaining term. You need this to even start.

**Step 2: Contact the servicer and request an assumption package.** This is official paperwork. It spells out what's required to assume. Takes about 5-10 business days.

**Step 3: Submit your application.** If you're a veteran, the VA will verify your entitlement. If you're a non-veteran, you'll go through standard mortgage underwriting. Credit, income, employment history, assets.

**Step 4: The lender reviews your creditworthiness.** They're checking if you can make the payment. This is serious. They'll deny you if they think you can't.

**Step 5: Clear any conditions.** They might ask for recent pay stubs, bank statements, letters of employment. Standard stuff but it takes time.

**Step 6: Get the assumption approval.** The lender says yes or no. If yes, you're moving to closing.

**Step 7: Close the loan.** Sign the paperwork, record it, fund it. The loan is now in your name.

The whole thing takes 30 to 45 days if everything goes smoothly. And "smoothly" is doing a lot of work here. There's always complications.

## Eligibility Checklist

**If you're a veteran:**
- Do you have available VA entitlement? (Use your VA eligibility letter to confirm)
- Is your credit score 620 or higher?
- Can you meet the lender's debt-to-income requirements?
- Do you have reserves or savings?

**If you're a non-veteran:**
- Do you have 3% to 5% down payment available?
- Is your credit score 620 or higher?
- Can you prove stable income for the past two years?
- What's your debt-to-income ratio?

Miss any of those and you're likely getting denied. The lender doesn't bend much here.
