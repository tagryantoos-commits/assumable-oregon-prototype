## How It Actually Works

Step 1. Find a property with a VA loan that's assumable. (Not all VA loans are, but most newer ones are.)

Step 2. Get preapproved to assume. Your lender verifies you're eligible (active duty, veteran, or surviving spouse), runs your credit, checks your income. It's similar to a normal VA approval but faster.

Step 3. Request assumption approval from the original lender. This is where it gets interesting. They'll look at your financials. If you qualify (and most military do), they approve it. The original lender still services the loan. Nothing weird happens.

Step 4. Close. You take over payments. The seller moves on. You're at their original rate.

Simple. Not instant. But faster than waiting for rates to drop 3%.

## What People Get Wrong

"Wait, don't I need the seller's permission?"

Yes and no. The seller's lender has the final say. But the seller's going to cooperate because they want to sell. They benefit too.

"Can I assume any VA loan?"

Not quite. The loan has to be assumable. Older VA loans sometimes aren't. But most VA mortgages written in the last 10 years are. I'd always verify first.

"What if the seller owes more than the house is worth?"

Then you're paying the difference in cash. That sucks. But that's rare in Colorado Springs unless they bought at peak and prices tanked. Not the market we're in.

"Does this affect my military benefits?"

Partially. Your VA benefit gets a marker on it showing you've used part of your entitlement. You can get it restored once the loan is paid off. So you can use it again later if needed. And if you're still active duty or a veteran, you can always get new entitlement once the first loan is gone. (This depends on when you served. Grab a tax return, your DD 214, and call me if you're confused.)
