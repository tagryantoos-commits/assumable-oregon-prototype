Alright, so here's the thing. Everyone's heard about assumable mortgages now. The word's out. But there are five specific plays that almost nobody's talking about. These aren't sexy. They don't make headlines. But they work, and they save people ridiculous amounts of money.

Let me walk you through them.

## 1. VA Loans with Non-VA Buyers (The Overlooked Gold Mine)

This one blows my mind because it's sitting right there and almost nobody uses it.

Here's the setup: A veteran owns a house with a VA loan at 2.4%. They sell to you. Non-veteran. Non-military. You assume that loan. Boom. You just locked in a 2.4% rate in a 6%+ world.

The catch? The veteran's entitlement stays tied up until you pay it off or refinance it conventionally. But that doesn't matter if you never plan to move.

Let me show you the math. Say it's a $350,000 loan at 2.4%.

Your payment: $1,386/month.

That same $350,000 at today's 6.5% conventional rate? $2,233/month.

You just saved $847 every month. Over 30 years, that's $305,000. Not in savings. In actual cash you get to keep.

The reason people don't do this? They think VA loans are "just for veterans." Wrong. The assumption is open to anyone. The VA doesn't care who buys the house. They care that the veteran's benefit is backed.

Most sellers don't know they can do this. Most buyers don't either. That's the gap.

**The play:** Hunt for VA loans in your market. Make offers. You'll find them. And when you do, you're looking at rates that closed three, four, sometimes five years ago.

## 2. FHA Assumable with Seller Financing (The Creative Stack)

FHA loans are assumable. Most people know that. But here's what they miss.

You assume the FHA loan (which has a lower rate than conventional). But the seller carries back a second mortgage for part of the down payment or gap.

Why does this matter? Because you just stacked two loans with terms the seller agreed to. And FHA loans sit at lower rates historically than anything else.

Example: House is $400,000. Seller's FHA loan is $340,000 at 3.2%. You assume that. Seller carries $30,000 at 4.5% for five years.

You bring $30,000 cash.

Your blended rate? Around 3.5%. Your payment? Manageable.

Without the assumable angle, you'd be looking at 6.5% conventional. Different universe.

The catch is that the seller has to be willing to carry back. Not all sellers are. But the ones who are? They get to stay in the deal longer, earn interest, and potentially avoid capital gains on the whole sale at once.

This is a negotiation. But assumable mortgages give you something to negotiate with.

## 3. Portfolio Loans (The Bank's Secret Stash)

Banks have portfolio loans. These are mortgages they keep on their books instead of selling to investors. Because they're held in house, they have way more flexibility.

Some portfolio loans are assumable. Some aren't. Most people don't even ask.

Here's the move: Find a home with a portfolio loan (usually from a regional or local bank). Call that bank. Ask if it's assumable. A lot of the time, it is.

Portfolio loans often sat in the books for years. The rate might be 3.8% or 4.1%. Lower than you'd get new.

The bank won't advertise this. Most loan officers don't know their own portfolio is assumable. You find it by asking. By digging. By treating it like a treasure hunt.

I've found portfolio loans that saved buyers $200K+ over the life of the loan because nobody bothered to ask the right question.

## 4. FHA to FHA Assumptions (The Easiest Assumable Play)

This one's actually simple.

FHA loans are assumable to anyone. You're a buyer. You assume an FHA loan. Refinance into a new FHA loan only if rates drop. Otherwise, keep the old one.

The reason this gets buried? It sounds boring. There's no "hack" or "loophole." It's just straightforward.

But straightforward saves money. FHA loans historically come in lower than conventional. You assume at the original rate. You're done.

The math is quieter but it's real. A $300,000 FHA loan at 3.5% versus 6.2% conventional is $105,000 in interest savings over 30 years.

**The play:** Stop filtering for "conventional only." Look at FHA assumptions. They're often cheaper and easier to close.

If you want to go deeper on how assumptions work across loan types, I've written about [can you assume a conventional loan here](/blog/can-you-assume-conventional-loan). Spoiler: way fewer conventional loans are assumable than people think. But FHA and VA? Those are your workhorses.

## 5. Assumable Loans in Rural or Secondary Markets (The Under-Hunted Territory)

People hunt assumable mortgages in hot markets. Denver, Austin, Nashville. But they ignore towns with 50,000 people where inventory sits longer and sellers are more motivated.

In rural areas, assumable loans are still assumable. But there's less competition to assume them. Fewer buyers know the trick.

I've seen assumable loans in secondary markets where the rate advantage is even bigger because older loans in those areas sit longer. And there's less buyer pressure because fewer people are chasing them.

This is a geographic play. But it works.

For example, I've written about assumable opportunities in [El Paso](/blog/assumable-mortgage-el-paso-texas), [Louisville](/blog/assumable-mortgage-louisville-kentucky), and [Savannah](/blog/assumable-mortgage-savannah-georgia). These markets have inventory that nobody's fighting over. And assumable loans sit even quieter.

## Why Nobody Talks About These

Most real estate content is written for the biggest markets, the hottest deals, the Instagram moments.

These five plays? They're unglamorous. They require asking uncomfortable questions. They need customization. They don't scale.

But they work. And they save real people real money.

The demand for assumable mortgages is [growing for a reason](/blog/growing-demand-assumable-mortgages). Rates are high. People are hungry for relief. These five opportunities are where that relief actually lives.

If you're hunting assumable mortgages and you're only looking at the obvious FHA lists, you're leaving money on the table.
