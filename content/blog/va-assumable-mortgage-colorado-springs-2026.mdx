# VA Assumable Mortgage Colorado Springs 2026: Military Homebuyers Guide

If you're a veteran shopping for a home in Colorado Springs right now, I need you to understand something. There are properties out there with VA loans locked in at 2.5%, 2.75%, maybe even 3%. And you can assume them. No new appraisal. No new underwriting. No PMI ever.

That's not an exaggeration. That's a real advantage that exists right now, and honestly, it blows my mind how few military buyers know about it.

Let me walk you through how this works, what it costs, and whether it makes sense for your situation.

## What Is a VA Assumable Mortgage?

Okay, so here's the baseline. A VA loan is a mortgage backed by the Department of Veterans Affairs. One of the perks built into VA loans is that they're assumable. That means if the current owner wants to sell, you can step into their mortgage. You take over their payment, their interest rate, their remaining loan balance. Everything.

Now, the current owner's VA entitlement (the benefit they used to get that loan) gets tied up until you pay off the loan or you assume it with your own VA entitlement. But the key point: you inherit their rate.

So if someone bought a house in 2021 at 2.65% and now you're buying that same house in 2026 when rates are sitting around 5.5%, you can assume that 2.65% loan instead.

Do the math. On a $400K loan balance.

At 2.65%: $1,659 a month (principal and interest).
At 5.5%: $2,271 a month (principal and interest).

That's $612 a month in your pocket. Over 30 years, that's $220,320 in savings.

And you don't pay PMI. Ever. That's another $300 to $500 a month you're not paying depending on your down payment.

## Why VA Loans Are Assumable (And Most Loans Aren't)

This is important context. Most mortgages today are not assumable. Your standard conventional loan, FHA loan, most USDA loans? The lender can call the loan due if you transfer the property. That's in the fine print. It's called a "due-on-sale" clause.

VA loans don't have that clause. Congress built assumability into the VA loan program as a benefit to veterans. It's a feature, not a bug. And it's been around since the GI Bill got started after World War II.

So when you're looking at properties in Colorado Springs, if it's got a VA loan on it, you can assume it. Full stop.

## The Process: How to Assume a VA Loan

Here's what actually happens when you assume.

**Step 1: Find the right property.** You need a seller with a VA loan. That's the starting point. Not every house has one, but there are more than you think, especially in Colorado Springs where we have a big military footprint (Fort Carson, Space Force, Peterson Space Force Base).

**Step 2: Get prequalified for assumption.** Your lender pulls your VA Certificate of Eligibility, runs your credit, checks your income. It's faster than a standard mortgage approval because there's no new appraisal needed. No new underwriting on the property itself. The house has already been appraised and underwritten once.

**Step 3: Submit the assumption request.** The VA servicer (the company managing the loan) reviews your application. They verify you have sufficient entitlement, that your credit is solid, that your income supports the payment.

**Step 4: VA approval.** Once the servicer approves, the VA issues a new Certificate of Eligibility that reflects the assumption. The seller's entitlement comes off the loan. Yours goes on.

**Step 5: Close.** You sign the paperwork, you get the keys, you start making payments at that locked-in rate.

Timeline? Usually 30 to 45 days if everything moves smoothly. Sometimes faster.

## What Does VA Assumption Cost?

Here's where people get confused. There are costs. But they're way lower than a standard mortgage.

**VA funding fee on assumption (if applicable):** The VA used to charge a 0.3% funding fee on assumptions. As of 2026, this can vary, so check with the servicer. On a $400K loan, that's $1,200. Could be rolled into the loan. Could be paid out of pocket.

**Assumption fee from the servicer:** Typically $300 to $500. Sometimes waived. Shop around.

**Title and recording:** Standard closing costs. Usually $800 to $1,500 depending on the county.

**Appraisal:** Not required. The existing appraisal stands. That's a savings of $500 to $700 right there.

**Underwriting:** Minimal. The property's already been underwritten. You're underwriting the borrower, not the property.

Total cost to assume? Usually $1,500 to $2,500 all in. Compare that to the $5,000 to $8,000 in closing costs on a standard purchase mortgage.

And remember. You're not paying PMI. You're getting a locked-in rate that's probably 2% to 3% lower than market. The math is ridiculous.

## The Catch (And There Are a Few)

I want to be straight with you. VA assumptions aren't a free lunch.

**You need VA entitlement available.** If you've used your VA benefit on a previous loan that's still outstanding, you may not have enough entitlement to assume. You can check your Certificate of Eligibility online through the VA website.

**The seller's entitlement stays tied up.** If the original VA borrower assumes another property later, they can't use that entitlement until your loan is paid off or you assume it with your own benefit. This doesn't affect you, but it affects the selling market. Some VA borrowers won't sell a property with an outstanding VA loan for this reason.

**Interest rate is locked, but taxes and insurance go up.** The mortgage payment stays the same. But property taxes increase. Homeowners insurance increases. Your total housing payment isn't frozen.

**Appraisal of value is the older appraisal.** If the property value has increased significantly, your loan-to-value might be lower than you think, which is fine. But you're buying based on the old appraisal, not the current market value. That can be a problem if the property has depreciated.

**The due-on-sale clause still applies to any second mortgage.** If there's a second mortgage on the property, the lender can call it due when you assume the first. You need to know this upfront.

## When VA Assumption Makes the Most Sense

You're a perfect fit for assuming if:

- You have available VA entitlement (check your Certificate).
- The property has a VA loan with a rate significantly lower than current market rates. (2.5% to 3.5% rates are golden right now.)
- You're planning to stay in the house for at least five years. The longer you stay, the more the rate savings compound.
- You can qualify for the payment based on your income and credit.
- You're comfortable with the existing loan amount. You can't easily add to an assumed VA loan.

It gets dicey if you need a bigger loan amount than what's on the property, or if the market rate is close to the assumable rate anyway.
