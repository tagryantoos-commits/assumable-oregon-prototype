# How First-Time Buyers Can Assume an FHA Loan in Colorado

Let me cut to the chase. If you're a first-time buyer in Colorado and you find a house with an FHA loan attached to it, you can take over that mortgage. You don't have to apply for a new loan. You assume the existing one. And if that loan has a rate below market (which right now, a lot of them do), you just locked in a way better deal than you'd get going the traditional route.

This is a huge advantage. And most first-time buyers have no idea it exists.

Here's what you need to know.

## Why FHA Loans Are Assumable

FHA mortgages are backed by the Federal Housing Administration. That's a government program. And because of that backing, these loans come with assumability built in. It's not optional. The lender can't say no.

Compare that to a conventional loan. Conventional loans are not assumable (in most cases). If you want to buy a house with a conventional mortgage, the seller pays it off, you get a new loan, and you live with whatever rates you're stuck with today.

FHA is different. The loan stays. You step into it. You keep the same rate, the same term, the same payment.

So if someone locked in a 3.2% FHA loan five years ago and you're assuming it today when rates are sitting at 6.5% or 7%, you're stepping into a deal that would cost you thousands extra per year if you went conventional.

## The Math That Makes This Worth Doing

Let's say you're buying a $350,000 house in Colorado Springs.

**Scenario 1: Conventional loan at today's rates (roughly 7%)**
- Down payment: $70,000 (20%)
- Loan amount: $280,000
- Monthly payment (PITI): Roughly $1,860/month

**Scenario 2: Assumable FHA loan at 3.8%**
- Down payment: $52,500 (15% for FHA assumption)
- Loan amount: $297,500
- Monthly payment (PITI): Roughly $1,390/month

That's $470/month you're saving. Over 30 years, that's $169,200 in savings.

And you put down $17,500 less cash upfront.

I'm not making this up. This is real money.

## The Step-by-Step Process for First-Time Buyers

Alright. Here's how this actually works in Colorado.

**Step 1: Find a property with an FHA loan.**

Not every house has an FHA loan. You need a real estate agent who knows what to look for. We can help with this. But the basic move is to ask the listing agent during your search: "Is this loan assumable?" If it's FHA, the answer is yes.

**Step 2: Get a pre-approval for the assumption.**

You'll need to contact the lender holding the FHA loan (the servicer, usually). You'll provide financial documents (income verification, bank statements, credit report). The lender reviews your application. They want to know: Can you actually pay this loan?

FHA assumptions are way less strict than getting a new mortgage. Your credit score doesn't have to be perfect. Your debt-to-income ratio is more lenient. But you do need to show you can afford it.

**Step 3: Make an offer that includes the assumption.**

In your purchase contract, you make it clear that you're assuming the FHA loan. You're not getting a new one. This protects you if something falls apart during underwriting.

**Step 4: Work with a title company.**

The title company handles the paperwork. They'll coordinate with the current lender, prepare assumption documents, and make sure everything is recorded properly in Colorado. It's not complicated, but it does require someone who knows what they're doing.

**Step 5: The lender approves the assumption.**

This takes 2 to 4 weeks typically. It's faster than a traditional loan. The lender is just making sure you qualify. They're not re-underwriting the whole property.

**Step 6: Close on the property.**

You sign the documents, transfer your down payment, and the loan transfers to your name. Done.

## What Down Payment Do You Need?

This depends on what's left on the original loan.

If the seller owes $250,000 and the house is worth $350,000, there's $100,000 in equity. You need to come up with cash to cover that gap, plus closing costs.

If there's not much equity, your down payment is smaller. If there's a lot of equity, your down payment is bigger.

On average, first-time buyers who assume FHA loans in Colorado are putting down 10 to 20%. It varies by deal.

## The Catch: You Need a Clear Credit and Income Picture

The lender will pull your credit. They'll verify your employment. They'll look at your debt. But honestly, the bars are lower than a traditional purchase.

You don't need a perfect credit score. You don't need 20% down. You don't need a 3.5 debt-to-income ratio.

But you do need to show you can actually afford the payment. If you're paying child support or student loans or have collections on your report, that will come up. The lender might ask questions. But they're not automatically disqualifying you.

## The Real Reason This Works

Here's the thing. The current owner locked in a low rate years ago. They're selling the house for market price today. But they're leaving that low rate on the table. It transfers to you.

That's the whole game. You're buying a $350,000 house but getting a mortgage payment based on 3.8% instead of 7%. The seller doesn't get to keep that rate advantage. The lender doesn't renegotiate. The loan just moves.

This is not something the lender likes to advertise. And it's not something most real estate agents push because there's less paperwork volume and less opportunity for them to earn more. But for a first-time buyer? It's one of the best moves in real estate right now.
