## Why FHA Loans Are Perfect for Assumptions (and First-Time Buyers)

Here's the thing about FHA loans: they're assumable. That means if you find a seller with an FHA mortgage, you can take over their loan. You don't reapply. You don't get a new rate. You step into their existing loan, their rate, their payment.

Right now, that difference is massive. A seller locked in their FHA loan at 2.5% back in 2020 or 2021. You're looking at 6.5% today if you go get a new mortgage. That's not a small difference.

Let's do the math. Say the remaining balance is $300,000.

At 6.5% over 30 years: $1,896 per month.
At 2.5% over 30 years: $1,061 per month.

That's $835 a month you save. Over 30 years, that's $300,600 in your pocket. And we're not even accounting for the fact that you pay off the principal faster at a lower rate.

For a first-time buyer, this is insane. This is the difference between affording the house and stretching too thin.

## The Basic Steps to Assume an FHA Loan in Colorado

Alright. Here's how it actually works.

**Step 1: Find a property with an assumable FHA loan.**

Not every house has one. You're looking for homes where the seller's mortgage is FHA-backed. Your real estate agent can pull this info. The title company will confirm it during escrow. Or you can ask the seller's agent directly: "Does this loan have an assumption clause?"

**Step 2: Get a pre-qualification from your lender.**

This is different from a new mortgage pre-approval. The lender needs to know you can qualify for the assumption. They'll check your credit, your debt-to-income ratio, and your ability to cover the down payment plus closing costs.

**Step 3: The lender requests assumption approval from the note holder.**

Here's where it gets real. The current lender (usually a big bank or loan servicer) gets to say yes or no. They're not required to approve you. Most lenders do approve FHA assumptions because it's less work than originating a new loan. But they still vet you.

They want to see:
- Credit score: 580 minimum (but realistically 620+ for approval)
- Debt-to-income ratio: Usually under 50% (some lenders go to 55%)
- Job history: Stable employment, ideally 2 years in your current field
- Bank statements: Proof you have cash reserves

**Step 4: Appraisal and underwriting.**

The lender will order an appraisal. The home has to be worth at least what you're paying. If it appraises low, you might need more cash down. Underwriting happens next. They'll verify everything. Tax returns. Pay stubs. Bank statements. It's thorough but it's faster than a full refi.

**Step 5: Close the assumption.**

Title company handles most of this. You sign assumption paperwork. You bring your down payment and closing costs. The note holder confirms the assumption is approved. You get keys.

The whole process typically takes 30-45 days if everyone moves fast.

## What Lenders Actually Want to See (First-Time Buyer Edition)

Look, I'm going to be straight with you. You're not starting from zero here. Most first-time buyers don't have a 750 credit score or 20% down. That's fine. Lenders know that.

Here's what they're actually checking:

**Credit score.** Aim for 620+. If you're below that, work on paying down credit cards before you apply. A 20-30 point increase takes 30-60 days sometimes.

**Debt-to-income ratio.** This is your total monthly debts divided by your gross monthly income. Your mortgage payment (including the assumed FHA loan) plus car payments, credit cards, student loans, all of it divided by what you make before taxes. Lenders want this under 50%.

Example: You make $4,000 a month gross. Your max debt should be $2,000 a month. If the assumed loan is $1,100 and you have a $300 car payment, you're at $1,400. You're in.

**Stable job history.** Two years in the same industry is gold. If you just switched jobs, that's harder but not impossible. Document the switch. Show your letter of employment from the new job.

**Cash reserves.** Have some money left after closing. Lenders like seeing 2-3 months of payment reserves. If your payment is $1,100, have $2,200-$3,300 sitting in savings after you close. This tells them you're not one emergency away from default.

**Down payment.** For FHA assumptions, you typically need 3-5% down. If the home is $400,000, that's $12,000-$20,000. Way less than a new FHA loan (which still requires 3.5% minimum anyway).

## The Down Payment Question

Here's what trips people up. You think you need to pay the full difference between what the seller owes and what the home is worth. You don't.

Say the seller owes $250,000 on their FHA loan. The home appraises at $350,000. You're not bringing $100,000 to closing.

You're bringing the down payment (3-5% of the purchase price in most cases) plus closing costs (usually 2-5% of the loan amount). That's it.

So in that $350,000 example, you're looking at $10,500-$17,500 down plus maybe $5,000-$8,750 in closing costs. Total out of pocket is roughly $15,500-$26,250.

Compare that to a new mortgage where you'd need $12,250 down plus closing costs anyway. The assumption doesn't cost you more down. It just gives you a way better rate.

## Common First-Time Buyer Worries (and the Real Answers)

**"Will the bank even approve me?"**

Probably yes if you hit the basics: 620 credit, 50% DTI, stable job. FHA assumptions are easier to get approved for than new mortgages because the lender's already got a performing loan. They want to keep it. You're not a new risk. You're taking over an existing one.

**"What if the seller still owes more than the house is worth?"**

That's called being underwater. The seller would have to bring cash to closing to cover the difference. Most underwater sellers just don't sell. Or they short sale. If you're looking at an assumption, the home's almost certainly worth more than what's owed. That's why the seller can sell it.

**"Can I assume a loan with bad credit?"**

Not easily. FHA itself doesn't block assumptions for people with lower credit scores, but individual lenders do. Some will go down to 580. Most want 620+. If you're below 620, work on it first.

**"How long does this take?"**

30-45 days if the lender moves. Some lenders are slower. Title company might need extra docs. But this is faster than getting approved for a new mortgage.
