Pueblo's a military town. Fort Carson is basically in the backyard. That means VA loans are everywhere here. And here's the thing that most people don't know: VA loans can be assumed. Which means if you're buying a house in Pueblo from a veteran who locked in a 2.5% or 3% rate five or six years ago, you might be able to step into that exact loan and skip the whole refinance nightmare.

Let me be super clear about what we're talking about. When you assume a VA loan in Pueblo, you're taking over the seller's existing mortgage. Same rate. Same payment. Same terms. The seller doesn't refinance. You don't apply for a new loan (well, mostly). You just slide in and take over.

The math here is wild.

Let's say there's a house in Pueblo selling for $280,000. The seller bought it in 2020 with a VA loan at 2.75%. Their payment is $1,187 a month (principal and interest).

Right now, if you go get a new conventional loan, you're looking at 6.5% to 7%. Same house. New loan. Your payment jumps to around $1,850 a month.

That's $663 more every single month. Over 30 years, that's $238,680 in extra payments. Just gone.

But if you assume that seller's VA loan. Your payment is still $1,187. You save $663 a month. $7,956 a year. Nearly $240,000 over the life of the loan.

And you're not the seller's lender hunting them down for anything. This is legal. It's built into how VA loans work. The seller can move on. You get the rate. Everyone wins.

Now. There's stuff you need to know.

## How VA Loan Assumptions Actually Work in Pueblo

When you assume a VA loan, you're not taking over their VA eligibility (that's theirs forever). You're just taking the loan. The process is actually pretty straightforward, but it requires some specific paperwork.

First, the seller needs to be willing. Most are. If they're selling, they're moving. They don't care about the loan anymore. They care about moving on.

Second, you need to qualify. The lender will pull your credit, check your debt-to-income ratio, verify your income. It's not as crazy as getting a brand new loan, but you do need to pass basic lending standards. Most people qualify.

Third, there's a VA assumption fee. Usually around $300 to $500. The lender charges it. Boom. Done.

Fourth, you might need to bring cash to cover the difference between the loan balance and the sale price. Let's say the seller owes $220,000 on that $280,000 house. You need to bring $60,000 down. That's the equity. The seller gets it. You own the house.

The whole process takes about 30 to 45 days, depending on the lender and how fast paperwork moves.

And here's something people miss. The seller's VA entitlement does get tied up during the assumption. But only temporarily. Once the assumption closes and you're officially on the loan, the seller's entitlement is released. They can buy another house with VA benefits. They're not stuck.

## What About Non-Veterans Assuming VA Loans in Pueblo?

This is the question I get all the time.

Can a non-veteran assume a VA loan? Technically, yes. The VA doesn't care who assumes it. The lender might care a little more (they want to make sure you can pay), but the VA itself is cool with it.

Now, here's the real talk. If you're not a veteran and you assume a VA loan, the seller's VA entitlement stays on the hook until the loan is paid off or you refinance. Which means if you default, the VA can come after the seller. That spooks a lot of sellers.

But it shouldn't. If you're assuming their loan, you're qualified. You're getting a credit check. You're getting an income verification. The lender is making sure you can handle it. The seller's risk is actually pretty low.

In Pueblo specifically, because there are so many military folks here, sellers get it. They understand VA assumptions. They're more comfortable with non-vet assumers than you'd find in other markets.

## FHA Loans: The Other Assumable in Pueblo

FHA loans are also assumable. If the house was financed with FHA, you can assume it too.

The process is similar to VA. You qualify. You bring money to cover the equity difference. You pay an assumption fee. You close.

FHA loans are a little different because the seller's FHA mortgage insurance doesn't go away. You're taking on that insurance premium too. Which means your monthly payment is a little higher than it would be otherwise. But it's still usually way lower than getting a new loan at current rates.

Let's do the math on an FHA example. House for $250,000. Seller bought it with FHA in 2019 at 3.5%. Their payment with mortgage insurance is $1,205 a month.

You get a new FHA loan at today's rates (roughly 6.25%). Your payment jumps to $1,695 a month. Plus insurance.

Assume that seller's loan. You're at $1,205. You save $490 a month. $5,880 a year. Over 25 years (common for FHA), that's $147,000 in savings.

The mortgage insurance stings a little, but you're still way ahead.

## The Stuff That Can Go Wrong

I want to be honest here. Assumptions aren't magic. They can fall apart.

The lender has to approve it. Most do, but not all. Some lenders have rules that make assumptions harder. They might require the assumption to be reviewed by underwriting. They might have specific debt-to-income caps. You won't know until you ask.

The appraisal has to come in at or above the purchase price. If the house appraised low, the lender might not approve the assumption. Then you're either renegotiating price or walking.

The title has to be clean. If there are liens or easements or weird stuff on the property, the assumption slows down.

And here's the big one. The seller has to close. If they get cold feet, if financing falls apart on their end, if they can't deliver clear title, the assumption dies. You need to make sure the deal is solid before you get too far into assumptions.

But these are problems with any real estate deal. Assumptions just add another layer to check.
