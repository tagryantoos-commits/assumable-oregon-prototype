Alright, so you're a military buyer in Colorado Springs and you've heard about VA loan assumptions. Maybe you found a house with a 2.65% VA rate and you're thinking, "Wait. Can I actually take over that loan?" Good question. Let me walk you through exactly who can assume a VA loan and what the VA actually cares about.

## The Short Answer

Yes, you can assume a VA loan if you're a military member or veteran with enough entitlement. But it's not automatic. The VA has specific rules, and they're stricter than most people think.

## Who Can Assume a VA Loan

This is the part where it gets granular. Ready?

**Veterans and active duty service members.** That's the primary group. If you served and got a DD-214 (or are currently serving), you're in the mix.

**Surviving spouses of deceased veterans.** If your spouse passed and they had entitlement remaining, you might be able to use it. This one comes with its own paperwork, but it's real.

**Non-military spouses of veterans.** Here's the tricky part. If you're married to a vet who's still living, you generally can't assume on your own entitlement. The assumption has to go through the veteran. It's their loan, their entitlement backing it.

Now, there's a fourth category that almost nobody talks about. Non-veterans (civilians) can assume a VA loan, but they lose the VA benefit. The VA won't back the loan anymore. The rate probably gets worse. The lender's approval odds drop hard. We see maybe a 20% success rate for non-vet assumptions, and that's being generous. So technically possible? Sure. Practically? I'd advise against it unless you've got a really specific situation.

## The Entitlement Piece (This Matters)

VA entitlement is the magic number. It's basically the government's promise to back your loan up to a certain amount. In Colorado Springs, that amount is important because home prices have climbed.

For 2025, the VA's basic entitlement is $36,000. But here's where it gets good. If you haven't used your full entitlement yet, you might be able to go higher. The VA can back loans way above the basic entitlement if the property value and down payment line up right.

So let me give you the real example. Say a house in Colorado Springs is listed at $450,000 with a VA loan at 2.65%. The seller's loan balance is $380,000. If you want to assume it, the VA needs to verify you have enough entitlement to back that $380,000. Most post-9/11 vets have that in spades.

But if you've already used entitlement on a previous VA loan, you need to know how much you've got left. That's your ceiling.

## Certificate of Eligibility: You Need This

Before anything else happens, you need a COE. Certificate of Eligibility. Without it, the VA lender won't even talk to you.

Getting one takes about two minutes online. You go to VA.gov, punch in your SSN and DD-214 info, and it spits out a PDF. You email it to the lender. Done.

If you're active duty, it's even faster. Your unit can confirm it.

Missing your DD-214? You can request it from the National Archives. Costs nothing. Takes a few days to a couple weeks depending on how backed up they are.

## Occupancy Requirements

Here's a rule that trips people up. The VA requires that whoever assumes the loan intends to occupy the property as their primary residence. Not a rental. Not an investment. Your actual home.

So if you're thinking about assuming a VA loan in Colorado Springs to rent it out, stop. The VA won't approve it. The seller's lender won't allow it either.

That said, life happens. You can move out later. But at the time of assumption, you've got to plan to live there.

## Credit and Income: What the Lender Actually Checks

The VA doesn't score you like a traditional lender. But the lender backing the assumption? They do. And they're careful with assumptions because they're taking on risk.

You'll need a decent credit score. I've seen assumptions close with 620. But honestly, 650 and above puts you in a way better spot. The lender's going to pull your credit and look at your debt-to-income ratio.

Speaking of DTI. Most lenders want to see you at 41% or lower. That means if you make $4,000 a month, your total monthly debt payments (mortgage, car, credit cards, the whole thing) should be under $1,640. The VA loan assumption payment has to fit into that.

Here's the thing, though. If you're assuming a VA loan at 2.65% and your current market rate is 6%+ (if you were buying new), your payment is going to be way lower. So even if your income isn't huge, the assumption might actually help your DTI.

## The Funding Fee (Sometimes You Pay It, Sometimes You Don't)

Not all assumptions require a funding fee. Let me break down when you do and when you don't.

If the seller is a veteran and they're using their entitlement to release theirs so you can take over, there's no funding fee. That's called a "full restoration of entitlement" situation. Clean.

If the seller isn't a veteran, or if they're keeping their entitlement intact while you assume, then you might owe a 0.3% funding fee. On a $380,000 loan, that's $1,140. Not killer, but it's there.

But here's the real talk. Most sellers don't want to stay liable on the loan. So they'll release entitlement. No fee. That's the standard assumption deal.

## Underwriting and Property Appraisal

Once you're clear on eligibility and you make an offer, the VA lender orders an appraisal. The property has to appraise at or above the loan balance you're assuming. If it appraises low, the deal gets weird. You either cover the gap with a down payment, or the deal falls apart.

In Colorado Springs' current market, homes have been holding value pretty well. But appraisals aren't guaranteed.

The lender will also verify the property is in decent shape. The VA cares about habitability. If the roof's about to collapse, you're not assuming that loan.

## Potential Deal-Breakers

Let me be straight. These will disqualify you or tank an assumption.

**Dishonorable discharge.** If your DD-214 shows a dishonorable discharge, the VA won't back you. There are a few other discharge statuses that disqualify you too. General discharge under honorable conditions is usually fine. But if you're unsure, check.

**Unpaid VA debt.** If you owe the VA money from a previous loan or overpayment, they'll offset it against your entitlement. Might not disqualify you, but it eats into what you can assume.

**VA loan already in foreclosure or default.** If you've defaulted on a VA loan before and haven't paid it off, the VA won't give you another one until you do.

**Property issues.** If the VA appraisal finds serious defects, the assumption stalls. The seller has to fix it, or the assumption doesn't happen.

**Lender's own rules.** Even if the VA says you're good, the lender backing the assumption might have stricter rules. Some won't do assumptions on rural properties. Some have their own credit score minimums.

## The Timeline You're Looking At

From offer accepted to closed, a VA loan assumption usually takes 30 to 45 days. Sometimes faster if everything's clean.

Compare that to a traditional sale where the buyer needs a new loan. That's typically 45 to 60 days. Assumptions move quicker because most of the underwriting is already done.

In a hot Colorado Springs market, that speed matters.
