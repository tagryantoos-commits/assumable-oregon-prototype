# How to Assume an FHA Loan in Colorado: Step-by-Step Guide for 2026

Alright. So you found a house with an FHA loan that you can assume. Rate's probably killer. Down payment's lower than a traditional mortgage. But now what? You're staring at the process and it feels complicated.

Let me walk you through this. I'm going to break down the exact steps you'll take from "I want this house" to keys in hand.

## Why This Matters Right Now

Here's the thing about 2026. Interest rates are still elevated compared to what sellers locked in during 2021-2022. A lot of those FHA loans are sitting at 2.5% to 3.5%. If you can assume one, you're Marty McFlying your way back to rates most people can't get anymore, even with perfect credit.

But the process has rules. FHA has specific requirements. Lenders have their own overlays. And most real estate agents don't know how this works because they don't do it often enough.

So let's be precise about the steps.

## Step 1: Find a Property with an Assumable FHA Loan

This is where it starts. You need to identify homes where the seller has an FHA mortgage and is willing to let you assume it.

Not all FHA loans are assumable. If the loan was originated before December 1, 1986, it's assumable with just lender approval. If it's newer than that (and most are), you need lender approval AND you have to qualify.

When you're looking at properties, ask the listing agent: "Is the FHA loan assumable? What's the original loan date?" Get that loan date first. Confirm with the current lender that the note actually allows assumption.

Your real estate agent should be pulling the loan documents (the promissory note) from title or asking the seller's agent for them. This document spells out whether assumption is permitted.

## Step 2: Get the Seller's Loan Information

You need details. Specific ones.

Ask the seller's agent or the seller directly for:

- Current loan balance
- Interest rate
- Original loan amount and date
- Current payment (principal and interest only, not PITI)
- Remaining loan term
- Lender name and loan number
- Any delinquency history (super important)

This information goes to your lender. Don't assume anything here. Call the servicer yourself if needed. You want to know if there are any issues before you invest time and money in an appraisal.

## Step 3: Find a Lender Who Does FHA Assumptions

Not every bank does this. Some lenders won't touch assumptions. Some have requirements that are so strict you'll never qualify.

You need a lender who understands FHA assumptions, has done them recently, and doesn't have unnecessary overlays.

When you call lenders, ask them directly: "How many FHA assumptions have you closed in the last six months? What's your timeline? Do you have any overlays beyond FHA requirements?"

Their answer matters. A lender who's done five assumptions is going to move faster and know the gotchas better than one who's done one in two years.

We typically work with specific lenders on assumable mortgages because we know their process and timelines. If you're in Colorado Springs or Northern Colorado, we can point you in the right direction.

## Step 4: Pre-Qualification and Financial Review

Your lender will run a preliminary check. They need to know:

- Your debt-to-income ratio (income divided by monthly debt obligations)
- Your credit score
- Your savings and reserves
- Your employment history

Here's what you need to know: FHA assumptions still have lending standards. You can't just assume a loan because it exists. You have to qualify. The bar is lower than a traditional mortgage (FHA is more flexible on credit scores and down payments), but it's still there.

The lender will also ask about the down payment. If the loan balance is $280,000 and the sale price is $320,000, you're putting down $40,000 (or 12.5%). That cash has to be yours. It has to be seasoned (usually in your account for two months already). And you need to prove it.

## Step 5: Make an Offer (with Assumption Language)

When you write your offer, your real estate agent needs to include language that makes the purchase contingent on your ability to assume the FHA loan.

Something like: "This offer is contingent on buyer's ability to assume the existing FHA loan at [loan balance] with [interest rate]. Seller agrees to cooperate fully with lender and provide all necessary documents."

This protects you. If the lender says no, you're not obligated to buy.

The offer should also specify who pays assumption fees (usually split or the seller covers it, depending on your negotiation).

## Step 6: Order the Appraisal

Once you're under contract, your lender orders an appraisal. The appraiser determines the property's current market value. This matters because your loan amount can't exceed the appraised value.

If the house appraises at $320,000 and you're assuming a $280,000 loan, you're good. If it appraises at $275,000 and you're assuming a $280,000 loan, you have a problem. The lender won't let you assume a loan that's larger than the property's value.

Timeline: Appraisals take 7 to 10 business days in Colorado right now.

## Step 7: Underwriting and Full Application

Your lender submits a full underwriting file to FHA (or their underwriting team, depending on the lender's setup).

You'll need to provide:

- Last two years of tax returns
- Last two months of pay stubs
- Last two months of bank statements
- Written explanation of any credit issues
- ID verification
- Authorization for employment and income verification

The underwriter is checking: Does this person qualify? Is the property safe? Is the loan assumable without issues?

If the current owner is behind on the loan (delinquent), the assumption is almost certainly going to be denied. FHA won't let you assume a delinquent loan. The seller has to bring it current first.

Timeline: Underwriting takes 5 to 10 business days, sometimes longer if there are issues.

## Step 8: FHA Assumption Approval (if required)

Here's where it gets specific to FHA.

Some lenders will do what's called "delegated authority," meaning they can approve the assumption themselves without sending it to FHA. Other lenders have to send it to FHA for review.

If it goes to FHA, expect an additional 5 to 7 business days. FHA's moving slower right now because volume is up.

FHA will issue an approval letter (or sometimes a conditional approval). This letter is what confirms: Yes, you can assume this loan.

## Step 9: Clear Any Conditions

If the underwriter or FHA issued conditions, you handle them now. Might be something like:

- "Provide letter of explanation for the 30-day late payment in 2023"
- "Increase reserves (savings) to meet FHA requirements"
- "Provide updated employment verification"

Most conditions are manageable. You respond within the timeline (usually 3 to 5 business days), and the underwriter clears them.

If a condition can't be met, the assumption gets denied. This is rare, but it happens. That's why the "contingency on assumption" language in your offer is critical.

## Step 10: Clear to Close

Once all conditions are cleared and the underwriter is satisfied, you get a "Clear to Close" letter. This means you're approved. The lender is ready to fund.

Your title company coordinates with the lender. You'll sign closing documents. The seller's lender gets paid off from the sale proceeds (if there's a down payment and closing costs coming from the sale price). Your lender funds the loan.

Timeline: Clear to close to actual closing is usually 3 to 5 business days.

## Step 11: Sign Closing Documents

This is straightforward. You show up at the title company (or do it remotely). You sign the assumption documents, the deed of trust, the promissory note (the assumption note, not a new one), and whatever other Colorado-specific documents are required.

The closing disclosure gets signed. The lender transfers funds. The title company records everything with the county.

## Step 12: Keys and Funding

Once the lender funds and the title company records, you own the house and you're now the borrower on that FHA loan.

The previous owner is released from liability (usually). You take over the payment.

Your first payment is typically due 30 days from the closing date.

## What's the Total Timeline?

From offer to keys: 30 to 45 days if everything is smooth.

If there are conditions or if FHA gets involved in the approval, add 10 to 15 days.

If the property appraises low or you have credit issues that need explanation, add another 5 to 10 days.

The slowest part is usually FHA's review (if delegated authority isn't available) and underwriting responses to conditions.

## What Gets Paid?

FHA assumption costs typically include:

- Appraisal: $400 to $600
- Title search and insurance: $600 to $1,200
- Lender's origination fee: usually 0.5% to 1% of the loan amount (sometimes waived)
- Underwriting fee: $300 to $500
- Processing fee: $300 to $500
- Assumption fee (to FHA or the lender): $0 to $300

Total closing costs for an assumption are often 1% to 2% of the loan amount. That's lower than a standard mortgage (which runs 2% to 5%).

For a $280,000 loan, you're looking at $2,800 to $5,600 in closing costs. The seller often covers some or all of this as part of the negotiation.

## Common Mistakes People Make

**Not confirming the loan is assumable before writing an offer.** You waste time. Write the offer contingent on assumption, but confirm assumability first via the lender.

**Assuming the interest rate will drop before closing.** It won't. You're assuming the rate that exists on the note. That's locked in. That's the deal.

**Underestimating how long underwriting takes.** Budget 3 to 4 weeks minimum, 5 to 6 weeks to be safe.

**Not having enough cash reserves.** Lenders want to see that you're not borrowing your down payment. If you're assuming a $280,000 loan and putting down $40,000, you need to show that $40,000 is yours, plus you have additional reserves (usually 2 months of PITI in the bank after closing).

**Working with an agent who doesn't understand assumptions.** This is huge. Most agents don't do assumptions. They don't know the timelines. They write offers without assumption language. Then everything falls apart.

Find an agent who gets this. Ask them if they've closed an assumption recently.

## Should You Assume an FHA Loan?

Let me be direct. If the rate is significantly lower than what you'd get on a new mortgage, and you qualify, and the property is sound, assuming is usually the play.

You're cutting 3 to 5 years off a new application. You're locking in a lower rate. You're paying lower closing costs. That adds up to real money.

But it only works if the loan is assumable, you qualify, and you have the cash for a down payment.
