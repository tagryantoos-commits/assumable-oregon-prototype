# How to Assume an FHA Loan in Colorado: A Step-by-Step Guide for Buyers

Here's the thing about FHA loans in Colorado. They're everywhere. And a ton of them are assumable. Which means you could step into a mortgage at 3.5% or 4.2% instead of fighting with today's rates. But most people have no clue how to actually do it.

Let me walk you through exactly how this works.

## What You're Actually Doing When You Assume an FHA Loan

First, let's skip past the confusion. Assuming an FHA loan means you're taking over the seller's existing mortgage. You're not getting a new loan. You're literally stepping into their shoes on their current note.

Why does this matter? Rate lock-in. If someone bought their Colorado home in 2021 at 2.75%, and you assume that loan, you get 2.75%. Not 6.5% or 7%. Two point seventy-five.

The math is wild. Let's say the home is worth $450,000 today. The seller's FHA loan balance is $280,000 at 2.75%. You assume that $280,000 at 2.75%. You come to closing with $170,000 down (the equity), and boom. You own the house at a rate most people are dreaming about.

Compare that to getting a conventional loan at 6.5%. Your payment on $280,000 at 2.75% is about $1,160 a month. At 6.5%, that same $280,000 costs you $1,775 a month. That's $615 more per month. Over 30 years, you're looking at over $221,000 extra.

Most people don't even know this is possible.

## Step 1: Find a Property with an Assumable FHA Loan

This is where it starts. You need to find a home where the seller has an FHA loan that's assumable. Not all FHA loans are assumable. Loans issued after December 1, 1986 (and that's basically all of them) are assumable. The catch? The seller has to be willing to let you assume it.

In Colorado, we're hunting for these properties. Some agents list them specifically as "assumable mortgage" properties. Most don't. That's why you need someone who actually knows what to look for.

The property itself can be anything. A condo in Denver. A ranch outside Colorado Springs. A townhome in Boulder. It doesn't matter. What matters is the loan underneath it.

When you're house hunting, ask your agent straight up: "Does this property have an FHA loan?" If yes, ask if it's assumable. Most of the time, the answer is yes.

## Step 2: Get Your Financial Ducks in a Row

FHA loans have qualification requirements. When you assume one, you have to qualify. This isn't a free pass. The lender (usually the original lender or a new one you bring in) will pull your credit, verify your income, check your debt ratios.

Here's what you need ready:

**Credit score.** You'll need a decent score. Usually 580 minimum for FHA, but lenders vary. If you're at 650 or higher, you're in good shape.

**Income documentation.** Last two years of tax returns. Recent pay stubs. If you're self-employed, prepare for more paperwork.

**Savings.** You need the down payment. Remember in my example above? That $170,000 in equity. That's cash or financing. Most buyers bring the equity in cash. Some bring part of it and finance the rest.

**Debt-to-income ratio.** Lenders want to see your total monthly debt payments (car loans, credit cards, student loans, plus the new mortgage) don't exceed about 43-50% of your gross income. This varies by lender.

Get these together before you're in contract. It speeds up everything.

## Step 3: Make an Offer and Write the Assumption Language

Here's where the legal stuff gets real. When you write an offer on an assumable FHA property, you need to include specific language that says you intend to assume the loan.

Your offer should say something like this: "Buyer intends to assume the existing FHA loan with a balance of approximately $280,000 at an interest rate of 2.75%. Seller shall provide Buyer with full loan documentation and payoff statement within 3 days of acceptance."

This does two things. It protects you. It tells the seller you're serious about this specific path.

Your real estate agent should know how to write this. If they don't, that's a red flag. Check out our guide on [can you assume a conventional loan](/blog/can-you-assume-conventional-loan) for more on how different loan types work.

## Step 4: Get the Lender Involved Early

This is critical. Contact the lender who holds the current FHA loan. Some lenders make this easy. Some are difficult. You want to know immediately which camp you're in.

Call them. Ask:

- Is this loan assumable without qualification?
- What's the current balance?
- What's the current interest rate and remaining term?
- What documents do you need from the buyer?
- What's your timeline for approval?

Write all this down. Get a name and direct number.

The FHA allows assumptions "with qualification" (which is standard) or "without qualification" (rare and usually only for owner-occupant family members). You'll almost certainly be doing "with qualification."

## Step 5: Get a Preapproval for the Assumption

This is where you prove you can actually assume the loan. You'll need to work with a lender (could be the current servicer, could be a new lender) to get preapproved for the assumption.

They'll run your credit, verify your income, check your debt ratios. Same stuff as a regular mortgage application, but the loan amount is what's left on the seller's note.

This usually takes 3-5 business days. Get it done right after you're under contract.

## Step 6: Order Your Title Search and Get the Property Inspected

You still need to do your due diligence on the property itself. This isn't different from any other purchase.

Order a title search to make sure there are no liens or issues with the property.

Get a home inspection. Assumable properties aren't always in better shape. You're getting a good rate. You still need to know what you're buying.

Get an appraisal if your lender requires one (most do). The home needs to appraise for at least the purchase price.

## Step 7: Review the Final Loan Documents

The lender will prepare assumption documents. This is basically a new promissory note and deed of trust with your name on it instead of the seller's.

Have a real estate attorney review these. Actually, have them review everything. Colorado has some specific requirements around real estate closings, and you want someone local who knows the state rules.

The documents will state:

- Loan amount (the balance, not the original amount)
- Interest rate (the original rate)
- Remaining term (how many years are left)
- Monthly payment
- Assumption fee (usually 0.5-1% of the loan balance)

Don't sign anything without understanding it completely.

## Step 8: Schedule Closing and Do Final Walkthrough

Once everything is approved, your title company or attorney will schedule closing. This is where it all comes together.

Before closing, do a final walkthrough of the property. Make sure the seller hasn't stripped anything out. Make sure nothing broke. Basic stuff.

At closing, you'll sign all the assumption documents, provide your down payment (equity), and take over the loan.

## Step 9: Get Your New Payment Set Up

After closing, contact the servicer and set up your payment. Don't assume your payment stays the same forever. Some lenders adjust servicing fees. Some don't. Get clarity on what your actual monthly payment will be going forward.

## What Could Go Wrong

I want to be straight with you. This process isn't always smooth.

**The lender denies the assumption.** This is rare, but it happens. If you don't qualify, they can say no. That's why preapproval is so important. Get it done early so you know if you're actually approvable before you're in contract.

**The seller changes their mind.** Less common, but if you're not under contract yet, they could refuse to cooperate. Always get this in writing.

**Title issues.** If there are liens on the property, the assumption can't move forward until they're cleared. This is why the title search matters.

**The appraisal comes in low.** If the home appraises for less than the purchase price, you might need to renegotiate or bring more cash. It happens.

**Closing delays.** Paperwork gets lost. Lenders slow down. Attorneys take longer than expected. Build in buffer time.

The key is knowing what can go wrong so you can plan for it.
