# How to Assume an FHA Loan in Colorado: Step-by-Step Guide

Alright, let's talk FHA assumptions in Colorado. This is one of my favorite strategies right now because the numbers are actually insane. You're looking at taking over someone's FHA loan that was issued before June 2009 (or after, with lender approval). But here's what makes this worth your time: older FHA loans have rates sitting at 2.5%, 3%, even 3.5%. Right now? New FHA loans are pushing 6%+.

That's not a small difference. That's potentially $200K in lifetime savings.

So let's walk through exactly how this works in Colorado.

## Step 1: Find an FHA Loan You Can Actually Assume

Not every FHA loan is assumable. This is the first filter.

FHA loans issued before June 1, 2009? Those are automatically assumable. Anyone can take them over. No approval needed from the lender. (Well, technically they need to sign off, but they don't have a choice.)

FHA loans issued after June 1, 2009? The lender has to approve you. It's way harder. Some lenders will do it. Many won't. It's case by case.

Here's what I do: I search the Colorado MLS for homes with FHA loans in the listing details. Then I pull the mortgage date. If it's before June 2009, we're golden. If it's after, I call the lender directly and ask if they'll consider an assumption. Sometimes they say yes. Most of the time they don't.

The older the loan, the better the rate. A 2012 FHA loan might be 3.75%. A 2005 FHA loan might be 2.8%. That difference matters over 20+ years.

## Step 2: Get Pre-Qualified for the Assumption

This is where the lender actually looks at you.

You'll need to provide:
- Last two years of tax returns
- Last two months of pay stubs
- Bank statements (60 days, usually)
- Employment verification letter
- Credit report authorization

The lender wants to see that you can handle the payment. They're checking your debt-to-income ratio. FHA is typically cool with up to 50% DTI, so you get some room.

Here's the thing though. If you have bad credit, this gets harder. FHA loans themselves have lower credit score requirements. But when you're assuming? Lenders get picky. I've seen lenders approve people with 580 credit scores on new FHA purchases. For assumptions? Some want 620+. Some want 640+.

Call the lender early. Don't wait until you're in contract on a house. Ask them straight up. "I want to assume an FHA loan. What's your minimum credit score? What's your max DTI?" You'll know in five minutes if this is even possible.

## Step 3: Make an Offer (With Assumption Language)

When you write your offer in Colorado, you need to be explicit about the assumption.

Your real estate agent should add language like: "Buyer is requesting to assume the existing FHA loan (Loan #[number], issued [date])." You'll also want an inspection period (typically 10 days in Colorado) and a financing contingency that lets you back out if the lender won't approve the assumption.

Here's where people mess up. They think the assumption is automatic once the offer is accepted. It's not. The seller accepting your offer is step one. The lender approving you is step two. If the lender says no, you need an out.

That contingency matters.

Also: Colorado doesn't require attorney review like some states. But you might want one anyway just to make sure the assumption language is tight. It's maybe $300-500 and saves headaches.

## Step 4: Order the Loan Assumption Package

Once your offer is accepted, the seller's lender will send you an "assumption package." This is the official application to take over their loan.

You'll fill it out. Submit it with all your financial docs. The lender will order an appraisal (you pay for this, usually $500-700).

Timeline? Usually 15-30 days to get a decision. Sometimes faster. Sometimes it drags.

During this time, don't make big financial moves. Don't rack up new debt. Don't switch jobs. Lenders re-verify everything right before closing. If something changed, they notice.

## Step 5: Handle the Assumption Fee and Any Equity Buyout

Here's what surprises people.

The seller has equity in the home. They paid down the loan balance. You're only assuming the remaining balance. To get the house, you have to pay them that equity difference.

For example:
- Home sells for $400K
- Loan balance is $280K
- Seller's equity: $120K
- You put down $40K (your down payment)
- You need a secondary loan (or cash) for the remaining $80K equity

That secondary loan is usually a personal loan, home equity line, or sometimes a new mortgage. It's not part of the assumption. It's a separate thing.

Also, FHA charges an assumption fee. It's usually 0.5% to 1% of the loan balance. On a $280K loan, that's $1,400 to $2,800. It gets built into closing costs.

## Step 6: Clear the Final Approval and Close

Once the lender approves you, you're in the clear. But they'll do a final verification of employment and credit before closing.

That's when they check one more time that you still have your job and you didn't go crazy with credit cards.

Closing is straightforward. You'll sign assumption documents. Transfer the title. Fund your down payment and the equity difference.

In Colorado, you'll also pay transfer tax (0.1% of the sale price in most counties) and title insurance. Nothing crazy.

One thing: the seller needs to sign off too. They're releasing the liability. Make sure they know they need to show up or sign documents remotely. Sometimes sellers ghost and it delays everything.

## Why This Actually Works (And Why More People Don't Do It)

I get it. This sounds complicated. Assumptions require more paperwork than a regular purchase. Lenders are slower. Not all sellers are willing to cooperate.

But here's what it looks like on the money side.

Let's say you're assuming a $300K FHA loan at 3% with 25 years remaining.

Your payment (principal and interest): $1,423 a month.

Compare that to a new FHA loan at 6% with 30 years.

New loan payment: $1,799 a month.

Difference: $376 a month. That's $4,512 a year. Over 25 years, you're looking at roughly $90K in savings.

And that's not even counting what you could do with that $376 every month. Invest it. Pay off other debt. Build a business.

Most people don't know assumptions exist. That's why more people don't do them. They see a home they like, they call a lender, and the lender gives them whatever rate is current. They never think to ask if the existing loan is assumable.

You now know better.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

Don't assume the seller will cooperate. Some sellers think the assumption is weird. Educate them. Show them the numbers. Make it easy.

Don't skip the pre-qualification call. Call the lender first. Ask hard questions. If they won't do assumptions, move on.

Don't forget about secondary loans for equity. You need cash or a backup loan ready to go.

Don't make big financial changes during underwriting. I'm serious. Keep everything stable until you close.

Don't assume you'll get approved. Have a backup plan. If the assumption falls through, what's your next move?
