# Step-by-Step Guide to Assuming an FHA Loan in Colorado

Assuming an FHA loan beats applying for a new mortgage in almost every way. You keep an existing rate. You skip months of qualification hell. You close faster. But the process is specific, and missing one step can cost you the deal.

Here's exactly how to assume an FHA mortgage in Colorado, from start to finish.

## What You Actually Need to Know First

FHA loans are assumable. That's the baseline. Not all loans are. If the property has an FHA loan, the next owner can take over the payments and terms without refinancing.

The catch: the lender has to approve you. This isn't automatic. You'll still need to qualify financially. But you're not starting from zero. The underwriting is simpler. The approval timeline is faster. And the rate stays locked in, which is huge if rates have climbed since the original loan was issued.

Before you do anything else, confirm the loan is actually FHA. Ask the seller for the mortgage note or deed of trust. Call the lender directly. Don't assume based on what someone told you.

## Step 1: Get the Loan Details from the Seller

The seller's lender holds all the information you need. Request these specifics:

**Loan balance.** How much is left on the mortgage.

**Interest rate.** This is the rate you'll assume. Lock this number in your head. If the current rate is 7% and the seller's loan is at 3.5%, you just saved yourself over 3 percentage points for the entire loan term.

**Remaining term.** How many years (or months) are left. A 30-year loan issued 8 years ago has 22 years remaining.

**Monthly payment.** Principal, interest, taxes, insurance (PITI). You need to know exactly what you're stepping into.

**Prepayment penalties.** Some FHA loans have them. Rare, but verify.

**Due-on-sale clause specifics.** FHA loans have a due-on-sale clause, but it's waived for assumable FHA mortgages. Confirm this language is in the note.

The seller's real estate agent or title company can pull this from the public record or seller's paperwork. Get it in writing. Don't rely on a phone conversation.

## Step 2: Determine Your Qualification Timeline

FHA assumption underwriting takes 2 to 4 weeks once you submit your application. This is faster than a standard mortgage approval, but it's not instant.

Get these items ready now:

**Two years of tax returns.** Personal and business if you're self-employed.

**Recent pay stubs.** Usually the last 2 months.

**Bank statements.** Last 2 months. The lender wants to see seasoned funds (cash you've had for at least 60 days).

**Credit report authorization.** You'll sign this when you apply. No surprises here.

**Proof of occupancy intent.** FHA loans are for primary residences. You'll need to sign an occupancy affidavit stating you'll live there.

**Employment verification.** The lender will contact your employer directly.

Have these assembled before you make an offer. When you go under contract, you're racing the clock. The faster you submit, the faster you close.

## Step 3: Make an Offer with Assumption Language

This is critical. Your purchase contract must include assumption language. It needs to state:

1. The sale is contingent on lender approval of the assumption.
2. The buyer assumes the existing FHA loan balance.
3. The seller is responsible for any prepayment penalties (though FHA loans rarely have them).
4. The purchase price reflects the assumed loan balance plus your down payment or closing costs.

Don't skip this step or word it casually. The contract protects you if the lender denies the assumption. It also clarifies what happens to earnest money and timelines if the assumption falls through.

Your title company will flag this. Make sure it's in the opening pages of your purchase agreement.

## Step 4: Order Title Search and Appraisal

You need to know if there are liens on the property. An FHA assumption doesn't erase other debts on the house (HOA liens, contractor liens, tax liens). Your title company handles this.

The appraisal is the lender's requirement. They need to confirm the property value supports the loan balance you're assuming. Most of the time, this is a formality. The home has already been appraised. As long as the value hasn't dropped significantly, you're fine.

Budget 10 to 14 days for both. Order them immediately after going under contract.

## Step 5: Submit Your Assumption Application

Contact the FHA lender directly or work through a loan officer who handles assumptions. Give them these items:

1. Completed Uniform Residential Loan Application (Form 1003).
2. Two years of tax returns.
3. Recent pay stubs and W-2s.
4. Two months of bank statements.
5. Signed occupancy affidavit.
6. Copy of the purchase contract.
7. Photo ID and Social Security card.

The lender will order the appraisal if they haven't already. They'll run your credit. They'll verify your employment. They'll check your debt-to-income ratio.

This is where the qualification piece happens. You need:

**A debt-to-income ratio under 43%.** Some lenders go to 50% in strong cases, but 43% is the standard. Add up all your monthly debt (car loans, credit cards, student loans, the new mortgage payment) and divide by your gross monthly income. If you're at 45%, you might get denied or asked to pay down debt before closing.

**A credit score of 580 or higher.** FHA's official minimum. Most lenders want 620+. Recent late payments or collections will slow things down or sink you.

**Proof of stable income.** If you've changed jobs in the last 2 years, have an explanation letter ready. If you're self-employed, expect deeper scrutiny.

The lender will give you a pre-approval estimate within 3 to 5 business days. This isn't a guarantee. It's conditional on the appraisal and final verification.

## Step 6: Get Clear to Close

Clear to close means the lender has verified everything and approved the assumption. You'll get this in writing. There's usually a list of final conditions (proof of homeowners insurance, final employment verification, etc.). Knock these out immediately.

Timeline: 2 to 3 weeks from application to clear to close.

Once you're clear, your title company coordinates the closing date. This is typically 5 to 7 days after clear to close.

## Step 7: Close the Deal

Closing on an assumption is simpler than a standard purchase. You're not getting a new loan. You're transferring the existing one.

You'll sign:

**Assumption agreement.** Confirms you're taking on the loan.

**Deed of trust or mortgage.** Transfers ownership to you.

**Title documents.** Proof you own the property free of other liens.

**Other paperwork.** Insurance docs, occupancy affidavit, any local Colorado requirements.

You'll pay closing costs. These are lower than a standard mortgage because there's no loan origination fee. Expect 2% to 3% of the purchase price for title, attorney fees, and recording.

You'll bring a cashier's check for your down payment and closing costs. Your title company will tell you the exact amount.

The closing process takes 1 to 2 hours. You walk out with the keys and the responsibility for the mortgage.

## Why FHA Assumptions Win in Colorado

Colorado's housing market moves fast. Assumable FHA loans give you a massive advantage.

First, you lock in an existing rate. If the seller's loan is at 3%, 4%, or even 5%, and current rates are at 7%, you've just cut your payment dramatically. On a $300,000 loan balance at 3% versus 7%, you're looking at roughly $1,100 per month in savings. Over 22 years, that's almost $290,000 in your pocket.

Second, you close faster. Assumption approval is 2 to 4 weeks. A standard FHA application is 4 to 6 weeks. That matters when you're competing for a property.

Third, you have fewer surprises. The property, the loan, the terms are all known quantities. You're not guessing. You're inheriting certainty.

The trade: you need acceptable credit and stable income. You need to qualify. You can't hide financial problems. But if your finances are solid, assumption underwriting is the easiest path to homeownership.

## Common Problems and How to Avoid Them

**The seller's lender drags on assumption approval.** This happens. Some lenders are slow. Push your loan officer. Escalate if needed. Most servicers have 30 days to respond to an assumption request by law. Don't let it slide.

**Your debt-to-income ratio is too high.** You knew this before you made an offer. If you're close, pay down credit card balances before applying. A $5,000 payoff can be the difference between approval and denial.

**The appraisal comes in low.** Rare on assumptions, but possible in declining markets. If the home appraises below the loan balance, you'll need to bring more cash or renegotiate the purchase price. This is why the purchase contract has an assumption contingency.

**You discover the loan isn't actually FHA.** Verify this before you go under contract. Don't waste time on a property with a non-assumable loan unless you're prepared to refinance.

**The title search finds a lien.** The seller is responsible for clearing this. Make it a condition of closing. Don't close with a lien on the property.
