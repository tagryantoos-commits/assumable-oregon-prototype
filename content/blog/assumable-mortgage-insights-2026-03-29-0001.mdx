# Assumable Mortgages in 2026: What's Changed and What You Need to Know

Alright. So we're a few months into 2026 and the rate environment has shifted. Not dramatically, but enough that people are asking me different questions about assumable mortgages now. "Is this still worth it?" "Are rates better now?" "Should I even look at assumables anymore?"

Let me walk you through where things stand and why, honestly, the strategy is actually more interesting right now than it was last year.

## The Rate Picture Today

First, let's get the baseline. We're sitting in a market where conventional rates are floating around 6.5% to 7%. Some days a little lower, some days a little higher. That's the market rate for a brand new mortgage right now.

Now, if you're looking at an assumable mortgage, you're buying someone's old loan. That loan was locked in years ago. Maybe it's a 3.2% FHA loan from 2021. Maybe it's a 2.8% conventional from 2020. Maybe it's a 4.1% VA loan from 2023.

Here's the math that matters.

Let's say you're buying a $450,000 house. With today's rates (let's call it 6.75%), your monthly payment on a 30-year loan would be around $2,970. Just principal and interest, no taxes or insurance.

Same house, but you assume a 3.5% loan with $300,000 remaining on it. Your payment drops to about $1,347 a month.

That's a $1,623 difference every single month. Over 30 years, that's $584,280 in savings.

Now, you do have to come up with a down payment to cover the difference between the loan balance and the purchase price. In this case, that's $150,000. But here's the thing: you're putting that $150,000 down, and your monthly payment is basically cut in half.

So the question becomes: "Do I have $150,000 down, or can I get creative with financing?" If the answer is yes, you're basically ignoring a massive financial win.

## What's Actually Changed Since Last Year

People ask me this all the time. "Ryan, rates have come down a little since 2024. Is assumable still the move?"

Yes. Actually, more yes.

Here's why. When rates drop, assumables become MORE valuable, not less. If rates are at 7% and someone has a 3% loan, that's a big gap. If rates drop to 6.2% and someone still has that 3% loan, the gap is even more attractive because the comparison to market rate is even better.

The other thing that's changed: more sellers are open to the conversation now. There's less FOMO in the market. Fewer people saying "I'm not even going to consider it." So deals are actually happening more often.

We're also seeing more creative financing structures. Sellers carrying back second mortgages. Down payment assistance programs that have loosened up a bit. Lenders getting better at underwriting assumables because, frankly, there are more of them happening.

## The Financing Question Everyone Asks

"Ryan, I don't have $150,000 sitting around. How do I make this work?"

Cool. Cool. This is where it gets interesting.

Option one: Save more. But you already know that.

Option two: Seller financing. The seller holds a second mortgage for some of that gap. You assume the first mortgage, put down what you can, and the seller carries the rest. This is happening more often than people realize. The seller benefits because they get payment from you, probably at a rate higher than what they'd get from a bank.

Option three: Down payment assistance programs. Colorado has a few. Some are specifically for assumables now. Check if you qualify based on income.

Option four: Combine everything. Assume the first mortgage. Put down $50,000 you have. Seller carries back $75,000 on a second. You get a smaller conventional loan for the remaining $25,000.

Is any of this easy? No. Does it require paperwork and patience? Absolutely. But the monthly savings are real enough that it's worth exploring.

## The Approval Part (Yeah, There's a Catch)

Here's the honest piece that I always lead with because people need to know this upfront. When you assume a loan, the lender gets to approve you. They're not just rolling over the old loan approval. They're checking your credit, your income, your debt-to-income ratio.

If you're buying a $450,000 house and assuming a $300,000 loan, your debt-to-income is way lower than someone getting a traditional loan for the full amount. That actually makes the approval process easier in a lot of cases, not harder.

But you still have to qualify. The lender will ask questions. They might want appraisals. They'll pull your credit.

For FHA loans, there's an additional step. The original borrower sometimes needs to be released from liability, which requires paperwork and lender approval. [If you want the step-by-step on how this works, I broke it down here](/blog/how-to-assume-fha-mortgage-step-by-step).

For VA loans, it's a little different. A VA loan can be assumed by pretty much anyone, but there's a certificate of eligibility piece and some specific VA requirements.

The point: it's doable. It's not automatic. Know the difference.

## Who This Makes Sense For in 2026

This strategy works best if you:

- Have solid credit and decent income documentation
- Can put down something meaningful (even if not the full gap)
- Are willing to be patient with the approval process
- Are buying in a market where assumables exist (spoiler: Colorado is loaded with them)
- Don't need everything to close in two weeks

If you're a first-time buyer with spotty credit and zero down payment, assumables might not be your lane right now.

If you're a savvy buyer who can move fast, has the down payment, and is willing to do the homework, assumables in 2026 are actually more accessible than they were last year.
